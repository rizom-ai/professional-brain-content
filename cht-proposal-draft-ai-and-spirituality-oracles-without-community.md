## Answers Without Practice: AI and the Rituals That Make Meaning

**Summary.** CHT's framing identifies two entangled phenomena: AI products moving into the spaces spiritual traditions have occupied, and an AI industry saturated with its own theology of salvation and transcendence. This project connects them through a single observation: spirituality is not primarily a set of beliefs or a stream of guidance — it is practice. Ritual, prayer, meditation, sabbath, pilgrimage, communal repetition: embodied, shared, deliberately inefficient activities that do not deliver meaning so much as cultivate the capacity for it. Practices are regenerative infrastructure — they renew attention, connection, and orientation toward what matters, the way maintenance renews a commons. AI spirituality products sell the *outputs* of practice — comfort, guidance, answers to existential questions — without the practice itself. The question this project asks: what happens to meaning when its products are decoupled from its practices? It extends the research program of *Institutions That Can Carry Themselves*: my Cognition proposal asks what happens when institutions outsource judgment; this one, what happens when people outsource the practices that make meaning.

**Research questions.**

1. *What practice regenerates.* Across contemplative and communal traditions, spiritual practice shares a grammar: repetition, embodiment, shared presence, deliberate friction. Drawing on practice theory and ritual studies, what do these practices actually regenerate — attention, community, orientation — and which of those capacities can survive when the practice is removed?
2. *The de-practiced product.* Bible Chat, Hallow's AI features, QuranGPT, Text With Jesus: which product features scaffold a person's own practice (habit support, community connection), and which replace practice with on-demand answers from a guide that stands in no tradition and answers to no community? Practice builds capacity through friction; retention products remove friction — under engagement economics the tension is structural, not accidental.
3. *The battle for attention.* Contemplative traditions are attention-training technologies; the engagement economy is an attention-harvesting technology. AI spiritual products sit exactly on that fault line: tools that promise contemplation, built on the economics of distraction. What would it mean to design for trained rather than captured attention?
4. *Transcendence without discipline.* Scholars have mapped Silicon Valley's salvation-and-transcendence rhetoric as a coherent ideology (Gebru and Torres's TESCREAL analysis; religious-studies readings of AI doom and accelerationism as competing eschatologies). This project adds the missing angle: every tradition binds transcendence to discipline — practice, community, obligation — while the industry's theology promises it as a product roadmap, shipped rather than practiced. What does that severance license in how AI is justified to the public?

**Why these questions are worth answering.** The migration is happening regardless of what anyone thinks about it: Bible Chat has passed thirty million downloads, Hallow has topped Apple's App Store charts, and QuranGPT strained under demand within a day of launch. If analysis stops at "people are praying to chatbots," it produces alarm without understanding. The practice lens explains *what specifically is at risk* (the capacities practices regenerate, which their products cannot), *why the substitution is attractive* (practice is demanding, products are effortless, and the communities that once carried shared practice have thinned — my white paper's central diagnosis, applied to meaning itself), and *what to do about it*: practice-support versus practice-replacement is a design distinction builders, buyers, and communities can act on. It also connects directly to my own work: ecosystem architecture is the design of practices through which communities renew trust, memory, and shared purpose. When I designed a collaboration vision for Planet B — a thought experiment in planetary-scale remote coordination — the question was precisely which practices hold a dispersed community together. Ritual is the oldest coordination technology we have; this proposal extends that work to the practices of meaning.

**Alignment with CHT's framing.** The de-practicing of spirituality is a predictable product of engagement economics entering a domain whose entire value lies in what engagement metrics cannot see. And it lands on CHT's founding terrain: the attention-economy critique, carried to attention's oldest training grounds. The trajectory is not inevitable, and the analysis equips communities of practice, secular and religious alike, to adopt technology deliberately rather than by default.

**Approach.** (a) Conceptual framework from practice theory, ritual studies, and philosophy — I was trained as a philosopher in the continental tradition and taught cultural analysis at the University of Amsterdam before spending a decade designing coordination and renewal practices for distributed communities and organizations; practice is my subject from both sides. (b) A design audit of leading spiritual AI products, mapping features to the practice-support/practice-replacement axis and to the retention incentives behind them. (c) Interviews with practice leaders in selected traditions — meditation teachers, chaplains, ritual designers, stewards of religious and secular practice communities — conducted with the cross-cultural care CHT's description asks for, and with candor about where religious-studies scholarship must be brought in.

**Deliverables (6 months).** Flagship essay *Answers Without Practice*; 2–3 op-eds (e.g., "You Can't Download a Sabbath," "Transcendence Is Not a Product"); a conversation on Your Undivided Attention or another CHT platform — the fellowship's podcast expectation — on why contemplative practice and the engagement economy are competing for the same resource: attention. As with my Cognition proposal, all work happens as open-notebook research in a [public, provenance-tracked knowledge base](https://yeehaa.io/dashboard), so every claim can be traced to its source.
