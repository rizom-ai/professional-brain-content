## Answers Without Practice: AI and the Rituals That Make Meaning

**Summary.** CHT's framing identifies two entangled phenomena: AI products moving into the spaces spiritual traditions have occupied, and an AI industry saturated with its own theology of salvation and transcendence. This project connects them through a single observation: spirituality is not primarily a set of beliefs or a stream of guidance — it is practice. Ritual, prayer, meditation, sabbath, pilgrimage, communal repetition: embodied, shared, deliberately inefficient activities that do not deliver meaning so much as cultivate the capacity for it. Practices are regenerative infrastructure — they renew attention, connection, and orientation toward what matters, the way maintenance renews a commons. AI spirituality products sell the *outputs* of practice — comfort, guidance, answers to existential questions — without the practice itself. The question this project asks: what happens to meaning when its products are decoupled from its practices? It is the second application of the research program developed in *Institutions That Can Carry Themselves*: my Cognition proposal asks what happens when institutions outsource judgment; this one asks what happens when people outsource the practices that make meaning.

**Research questions.**

1. *What practice regenerates.* Across contemplative and communal traditions, spiritual practice shares a grammar: repetition, embodiment, shared presence, deliberate friction. Drawing on practice theory and ritual studies, what do these practices actually regenerate — attention, community, orientation — and which of those capacities can survive when the practice is removed and only its outputs remain?
2. *The de-practiced product.* Bible Chat, Hallow's AI features, QuranGPT, Text With Jesus: which product features scaffold a person's own practice (habit support, community connection), and which replace practice with on-demand answers from a guide that stands in no tradition and answers to no community? Under engagement economics, which design wins? Practice builds capacity through friction; retention products remove friction — the tension is structural, not accidental.
3. *The battle for attention.* Contemplative traditions are, among other things, attention-training technologies; the engagement economy is an attention-harvesting technology. AI spiritual products sit exactly on that fault line. What happens when the tools that promise contemplation are built on the economics of distraction — and what would it mean to design for trained rather than captured attention?
4. *Transcendence without discipline.* Scholars have mapped Silicon Valley's salvation-and-transcendence rhetoric as coherent ideology — Gebru and Torres's TESCREAL analysis, and religious-studies work reading AI doom and accelerationism as competing eschatologies. This project builds on that scholarship from a different angle: every tradition binds transcendence to discipline — practice, community, obligation. The industry's theology promises transcendence as a product roadmap: something shipped, not practiced. What does that severance license in how AI is built and justified to the public?

**Why these questions are worth answering.** The migration is happening regardless of what anyone thinks about it: Bible Chat has passed thirty million downloads, Hallow has topped Apple's App Store charts, and QuranGPT strained under demand within a day of launch. If analysis stops at "people are praying to chatbots," it produces alarm without understanding. The practice lens explains *what specifically is at risk* (the capacities practices regenerate, which their products cannot), *why the substitution is attractive* (practice is demanding, products are effortless, and the communities that once carried shared practice have thinned — my white paper's central diagnosis, here applied to meaning itself), and *what to do about it*: the distinction between technology that supports practice and technology that replaces it is a design distinction, actionable by builders, buyers, and communities alike. This is also where the project connects to my own work. Ecosystem architecture, as I practice it, is the design of the practices through which communities renew trust, memory, and shared purpose — when I was commissioned to design a collaboration vision for Planet B, a thought experiment in planetary-scale remote coordination, the question was precisely which practices hold a dispersed community together. Ritual is the oldest coordination technology we have. This proposal extends that work from organizations to the practices of meaning.

**Alignment with CHT's framing.** The de-practicing of spirituality is a predictable product of engagement economics entering a domain whose entire value lies in what engagement metrics cannot see. And it lands on CHT's founding terrain: the attention economy critique, carried to attention's oldest training grounds. The trajectory is not inevitable — practice-support versus practice-replacement is a concrete design axis, and the analysis equips communities of practice, secular and religious alike, to adopt technology deliberately rather than by default.

**Approach.** (a) Conceptual framework from practice theory, ritual studies, and philosophy — I was trained as a philosopher in the continental tradition and taught cultural analysis at the University of Amsterdam before spending a decade designing coordination and renewal practices for distributed communities and organizations; practice is my subject from both sides. (b) A design audit of leading spiritual AI products, mapping features to the practice-support/practice-replacement axis and to the retention incentives behind them. (c) Interviews with practice leaders in selected traditions — meditation teachers, chaplains, ritual designers, stewards of religious and secular practice communities — conducted with the cross-cultural care CHT's description asks for, and with honesty about where my competence ends and religious-studies scholarship must be brought in.

**Deliverables (6 months).** Flagship essay *Answers Without Practice*; 2–3 op-eds (e.g., "You Can't Download a Sabbath," "Transcendence Is Not a Product"); a conversation on Your Undivided Attention or another CHT platform — the fellowship's podcast expectation — on why contemplative practice and the engagement economy are competing for the same resource: attention. As with my Cognition proposal, the work will be conducted as open-notebook research in a [public, provenance-tracked knowledge base](https://yeehaa.io/dashboard), so every claim about what these products do can be traced to its source.
