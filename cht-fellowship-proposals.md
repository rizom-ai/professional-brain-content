# CHT Fellowship Proposals

## Proposal 1: The Outsourced Institution

### What Happens When Organizations Offload Memory and Judgment to AI

**Summary.** In *Institutions That Can Carry Themselves* (2026), I describe a foundation whose fluent AI assistant could summarize every program and find every budget, yet could not explain why one project was renewed while another quietly disappeared. The institution had preserved everything except its reasons. CHT asks whether AI is more like a calculator or something qualitatively different for human thinking. I propose to answer that question at a level the current debate almost entirely ignores: the institution. Humans have never thought alone — we think through organizations, archives, roles, and procedures that remember, judge, and decide on our behalf. Institutions are now offloading their cognition to AI faster than any individual, and we have almost no rigorous account of what that does to collective judgment. This project extends the cognitive-offloading question from the individual mind to the institutional one, and asks: what happens to a society when its organizations can retrieve everything and remember nothing?

**Research questions.**

1. *Retrieval versus memory.* Institutional memory is not storage; it is the capacity to make the past available to present judgment — knowing which decisions are still alive, which documents are ceremonial, which exceptions became practice. When AI summarization and retrieval substitute for that capacity, what exactly is lost? Can the difference be made observable?
2. *Fluency versus judgment.* AI compresses weak institutional context into confident, fluent output. Under what conditions does that fluency displace the deliberation it imitates — and make institutional gaps harder to see and harder to contest?
3. *Cognitive monoculture.* CHT's description asks what it means for diversity of thought when hundreds of millions of people rely on the same small set of models. The institutional version is sharper: when thousands of organizations route their internal sense-making through identical foundation models, do their judgments converge? What are the systemic risks of correlated institutional cognition — in markets, public administration, media?
4. *The disappearing apprenticeship.* Institutional judgment has historically been transmitted through the humans who carried it: veterans, fixers, coordinators. If AI absorbs their tasks without absorbing their context, how does the next generation of institutional judgment form at all? This is the adults-versus-kids question in organizational form: what happens to those who never practice the delegated task?

**Why these questions are worth answering.** The individual level now has real findings: search access reshapes what people internalize versus where they expect to find it (Sparrow, Liu & Wegner, *Science* 2011); heavier reliance on AI tools correlates with weaker critical-thinking performance (Gerlich 2025); knowledge workers report substantially less critical-thinking effort on AI-assisted tasks, especially when their confidence in the tool is high (Lee et al., CHI 2025); and EEG work on LLM-assisted writing finds reduced neural engagement and lower ownership of the result (Kosmyna et al. 2025). The institutional level has almost none of this, even though most consequential decisions — medical, financial, governmental, educational — are made by institutions, not individuals. My working hypothesis, developed in the white paper, is that AI functions as an *institutional stress test*: as a transactive partner every member can query, it rewrites the group's directory of who-knows-what — exposing and amplifying cognitive decay rather than creating it, and compressing missing context into plausible output. If that is right, harms will surface not as dramatic model failures but as quiet erosion of institutions' capacity to explain, contest, and stand behind their own decisions — a mechanism for institutional distrust usually attributed to politics alone. The field currently runs on anecdote; distinguishing what we know from what we suspect is precisely the work.

**Alignment with CHT's framing.** These outcomes are not inevitable properties of the technology. They are predictable products of design choices: chat interfaces that present synthesis without provenance or status; business models: per-seat productivity tools optimized for output volume, not decision quality; and institutional incentives: throughput metrics that reward fast fluent answers over contestable ones. Naming the mechanism creates the possibility of different choices — interfaces that carry provenance, deployment norms that protect deliberation, procurement standards that ask what a tool does to judgment rather than only to throughput.

**Approach.** (a) A conceptual framework organized around transactive memory theory (Wegner) — the established account of how groups remember by distributing knowledge across members and maintaining a shared directory of who knows what — extended to treat AI as a new transactive partner that rewrites that directory. This is the framework I already apply through Rizom's transactive-memory diagnostics with organizations, and it is the natural bridge from individual offloading to the institutional kind. Distributed cognition (Hutchins) situates it in the wider tradition of thinking that happens across people and tools; my own distinctions — storage versus memory, fluency versus judgment — supply the philosophical edge the empirical literature lacks. (b) Qualitative case studies: structured interviews with practitioners in 6–10 organizations that have deployed AI assistants over internal knowledge (a public agency, a foundation, a mid-size company, a newsroom), tracing concrete decisions before and after adoption. My own deployments give me instrumented access to organizations mid-offloading — a stated interest as much as an advantage, which is why the case set will be balanced with organizations I have no commercial relationship with. (c) A synthesis of the emerging empirical literature on cognitive offloading, honestly marking where individual-level findings do and do not transfer to institutions.

**Deliverables (6 months).** Flagship report *The Outsourced Institution* — framework, case evidence, and a practical diagnostic ("is this system extending our judgment or replacing it?"); 2–3 op-eds (e.g., "Your Company Doesn't Remember Anything Anymore," "The Coming Monoculture of Institutional Judgment"); a conversation on Your Undivided Attention or another CHT platform — the fellowship's podcast expectation — on why AI's real cognitive impact may run through our institutions rather than our brains. The research will be conducted as open-notebook research in a [public, provenance-tracked knowledge base](https://yeehaa.io/dashboard) — the kind of [infrastructure I build in my day job](https://github.com/rizom-ai/brains) — so any reader can trace every claim in the flagship report to its source; the method practices what the report argues.

**Fit.** Former assistant professor (University of Amsterdam) turned founder; a decade building knowledge and learning infrastructure for organizations, most recently at Rizom. I have watched the offloading happen from inside the deployments, and I can translate between the philosophy of cognition, the empirical literature, and what practitioners actually experience.

## Proposal 2: Responsibility Laundering

### How AI Agents Are Built to Act Without Answering — and the Open-Source Alternative

**Summary.** The industry is racing to deploy AI agents: systems that don't just answer questions but retrieve, draft, route, prioritize, and act inside organizations. I build these systems — I maintain an open-source agent framework and deploy it with real organizations — which gives me firsthand knowledge of the layer this focus area asks about: how agentic AI is actually wired together, how capability claims survive contact with a workplace, and what drives the design decisions that ship. From that seat, the central finding is this: today's agents are institutionally ambiguous *by design*. They sound like analysts, act like coordinators, influence like managers, and disappear like tools. When something goes wrong there is no role that was exceeded, no provenance to inspect, no path for an affected person to contest the output. I call this *responsibility laundering*: agency passes through the machine and comes out clean. This is the companion to the Cognition proposal — where that asks what happens to institutional memory and judgment when they are outsourced to AI, this asks what happens to responsibility when action is. The project pairs an insider's anatomy of how laundering happens with what critique alone cannot supply: a working, open-source proof that the accountable alternative is buildable. CHT asks what it would take to shift the incentives shaping this technology from the inside out. One answer: build the accountable version in the open, publish the standard, and give it away.

**Research questions.**

1. *The anatomy of responsibility laundering.* The accountability gap is not new — Nissenbaum named the "problem of many hands" and the "computer as scapegoat" in 1996; Elish's "moral crumple zone" (2019) showed how a token human absorbs blame for a system they could not control. Responsibility laundering names what agentic AI adds: design choices that make the gap routine and by-design rather than exceptional. Which choices? From practice: context-free assistant personas that speak from nowhere; synthesis that strips provenance and source status; permission models that bound what an agent can *access* but never what it is *for*; the missing contestation path. Even when a human approves the output, the agent has already shaped the frame in which the decision became visible — approval becomes the crumple zone.
2. *The capability-claims pipeline.* How does a benchmark result become a sales deck, a procurement decision, and a deployment mandate — and where does critical evaluation fail? The gap is now documented: MIT's 2025 *State of AI in Business* found 95% of enterprise generative-AI pilots deliver no measurable P&L impact; RAND reports over 80% of AI projects fail, roughly twice the rate of non-AI IT. This is CHT's question about evaluating capability claims, answered from the layer where the claims land: what a benchmark-topping agent actually does inside a municipality, a foundation, a company.
3. *The economics that select these defaults.* Accountability is friction, and friction loses procurement battles: general frictionless assistants demo better, sell faster, and deploy cheaper than bounded, contestable ones. And the failures above are rebranded rather than reported, so the "adopt or fall behind" narrative never meets its own base rates. This is the demand-side complement to the financial architecture CHT's description names — the venture and vendor incentives make sense only once you see what buyers are told and what failures are quietly absorbed.
4. *The buildable alternative — with receipts.* My white paper specifies *situated agents*: named role, bounded scope, provenance-carrying output, explicit escalation and contestation paths. I am building this pattern as [open-source infrastructure](https://github.com/rizom-ai/brains) and running it with real organizations. This project turns that practice into a published design standard precise enough for an engineer to implement and a procurement officer to require — the existence proof that the current defaults are choices, not physics.

**Why these questions are worth answering.** The public conversation about AI agents runs on vendor claims and lab announcements. The layer where those claims become decisions about real people — who gets flagged, prioritized, denied, or framed — is almost undocumented, because the people who see it are selling into it. Most near-term AI harm to ordinary people will be administered through this layer, not through frontier-model misbehavior. Naming the mechanism — responsibility laundering, situated agents, ghost managers — gives journalists, regulators, and buyers handles they lack; a working reference implementation gives builders something to copy and buyers something to demand.

**Alignment with CHT's framing.** This is "harms are predictable products of business models and design choices" with a paper trail: this feature exists because it demos well; this accountability gap exists because closing it slows the sale. The constructive orientation is structural, not rhetorical — the deliverable is a standard plus running code, which is what "the trajectory is not inevitable" looks like when a technologist says it. Disclosure is part of the design: I sell in this layer, which is exactly why the analysis runs in the open where every claim can be checked. And I should be direct about positioning — I am not a frontier-lab insider. I am an insider of the layer where the frontier's claims are monetized and where the public's actual encounter with AI is decided; that is the technologist perspective this focus area most lacks.

**Approach.** (a) A technical anatomy of leading agent frameworks and enterprise assistant products, mapping each accountability gap to the design decision that produces it — engaging the accountability-gap literature (Nissenbaum, Elish) and written to be legible to non-engineers without dumbing down the engineering. (b) Interviews across the deployment ecosystem — engineers shipping agentic systems, implementation consultants, buyers — on the incentives that shape defaults and bury failures. (c) The situated-agent design standard, published openly with the reference implementation.

**Deliverables (6 months).** Flagship report *Responsibility Laundering*, closing with a published situated-agent specification; 2–3 op-eds (e.g., "Your New AI Coworker Has No Job Description," "Nobody Reports a Failed AI Project"); a conversation on Your Undivided Attention or another CHT platform — the fellowship's podcast expectation — tracing how one agent-shaped decision travels through an organization and where answerability leaks out. All work happens as open-notebook research in a [public, provenance-tracked knowledge base](https://yeehaa.io/dashboard) running on the same open-source infrastructure the project examines — the research is itself a live demonstration of the design standard it proposes.

**Fit.** A decade building knowledge and coordination infrastructure for organizations, most recently at Rizom, where I develop and deploy the agent systems this proposal examines; before that, an assistant professor at the University of Amsterdam. I move between the engineering of these systems and the philosophical questions about authority and responsibility they raise.
