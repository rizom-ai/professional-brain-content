# CHT Fellowship Proposals

These are related but distinct projects. Proposal 1 examines institutional memory and judgment: what happens when organizations outsource the work by which they remember and decide. Proposal 2 examines responsibility when AI acts: how agency and accountability are distributed when agents operate inside organizations, and what an accountable alternative might look like.

## Proposal 1: The Outsourced Institution

### What Happens When Organizations Offload Memory and Judgment to AI

**Summary.** Humans think through organizations, archives, roles, and procedures that remember, judge, and decide on our behalf. Institutions are now offloading that cognition to AI faster than any individual, and we have almost no rigorous account of what that does to collective judgment. This project asks: what happens to a society when its organizations can retrieve everything and remember nothing?

**Research questions.**

1. *Retrieval versus memory.* Institutional memory is not storage; it is the capacity to make the past available to present judgment — knowing which decisions are still alive, which documents are ceremonial, which exceptions became practice. When AI summarization and retrieval substitute for that capacity, what exactly is lost? Can the difference be made observable?

2. *Fluency versus judgment.* AI compresses weak institutional context into confident, fluent output. Under what conditions does that fluency displace the deliberation it imitates — and make institutional gaps harder to see and harder to contest?

3. *Cognitive monoculture.* When thousands of organizations route their internal sense-making through identical foundation models, do their judgments converge? What are the systemic risks of correlated institutional cognition, and how does this affect markets, public administration, and media?

**Why these questions are worth answering.** The individual level now has real findings: search access reshapes what people internalize versus where they expect to find it (Sparrow, Liu & Wegner, *Science* 2011), while knowledge workers report less critical-thinking effort on AI-assisted tasks, especially when their confidence in the tool is high (Lee et al., CHI 2025). The institutional level has almost none of this, even though most consequential decisions — medical, financial, governmental, educational — are made by institutions, not individuals.

There is also a generational consequence. Institutional judgment has historically been transmitted through the humans who carried it: veterans, fixers, and coordinators. If AI absorbs their tasks without absorbing their context, the next generation may never practice the delegated work from which judgment is formed. The question is not only what current institutions lose, but how future institutions learn to judge at all.

My working hypothesis is that AI functions as an *institutional stress test*: as a transactive partner every member can query, it rewrites the group's directory of who-knows-what, exposing and amplifying cognitive decay rather than creating it, and compressing missing context into plausible output. If that is right, harms will surface not as dramatic model failures but as quiet erosion of institutions' capacity to explain, contest, and stand behind their own decisions. The field currently runs on anecdote; distinguishing what we know from what we suspect is precisely the work.

The inquiry will also examine how design choices, business models, and institutional incentives shape these outcomes. Interfaces that present synthesis without provenance or status, productivity tools optimized for output volume, and throughput metrics that reward fast fluent answers over contestable ones all make certain forms of offloading more likely. Naming those mechanisms creates the possibility of different choices: interfaces that carry provenance, deployment norms that protect deliberation, and procurement standards that ask what a tool does to judgment rather than only to throughput.

**Approach.** This will be a practice-based inquiry grounded in the concrete deployments and decisions through which institutions offload memory and judgment. As a philosopher-builder, I will trace what changes when an organization introduces AI into its knowledge work: what gets made retrievable, what gets summarized away, which exceptions remain visible, who is expected to remember the reasons, and how decisions are subsequently explained or contested. I can examine these changes from inside the technical work — through the systems I build and deploy, the design choices they encode, and the institutional conversations around their adoption — while remaining attentive to the philosophical distinctions those changes put under pressure.

The inquiry will develop and test two distinctions in situated technical experiments: storage versus memory, and fluency versus judgment. These are working questions to be tested against real interfaces, records, workflows, and decisions, not predetermined categories imposed on organizations. Open infrastructure will make the experiments inspectable and revisable. The work will follow concrete episodes across deployments and decisions rather than an artificial fixed interview count, building a body of evidence rich enough to show where the distinctions hold, where they fail, and what institutional alternatives might look like.

Transactive memory theory (Wegner) will be the primary conceptual framework. Distributed cognition (Hutchins) provides a supporting lineage for understanding institutional thinking across people, tools, and environments. Both will serve as interpretive frameworks rather than as a theory-led research design. The emerging empirical literature on cognitive offloading will be brought into conversation with the cases, with care about where individual-level findings do and do not transfer to institutions.

**Deliverables (6 months).** Flagship report *The Outsourced Institution* — framework, case evidence, and a practical diagnostic: “Is this system extending our judgment or replacing it?”; 2–3 op-eds (e.g., “Your Company Doesn't Remember Anything Anymore,” “The Coming Monoculture of Institutional Judgment”); and a conversation on *Your Undivided Attention* or another CHT platform — the fellowship's podcast expectation — on why AI's real cognitive impact may run through our institutions rather than our brains. The research will be conducted as open-notebook research in a [public, provenance-tracked knowledge base](https://yeehaa.io/dashboard) — the kind of [infrastructure I build in my day job](https://github.com/rizom-ai/brains) — so any reader can trace every claim in the flagship report to its source; the method practices what the report argues.

## Proposal 2: Responsibility Laundering

### How AI Agents Are Built to Act Without Answering — and the Open-Source Alternative

**Summary.** I build and deploy AI agents inside organizations. I maintain an open-source agent framework and deploy it with real organizations, which gives me firsthand knowledge of how agentic AI is wired together, how capability claims survive contact with a workplace, and what drives the design decisions that ship. From that layer, the industry-wide pattern becomes visible: agents retrieve, draft, route, prioritize, and act, but their institutional role remains ambiguous. They sound like analysts, act like coordinators, influence like managers, and disappear like tools. When something goes wrong, there is no role that was exceeded, no provenance to inspect, and no clear path for an affected person to contest the output. I call this *responsibility laundering*: agency passes through the machine and comes out clean.

This project asks what happens to responsibility when action is delegated. It pairs an insider's anatomy of how laundering happens with what critique alone cannot supply: a working, open-source proof that an accountable alternative is buildable. The running reference implementation is central to the project: build the accountable version in the open, publish the standard, and give it away.

**Research questions.**

1. *The anatomy of responsibility laundering.* Nissenbaum named the “problem of many hands” and the “computer as scapegoat” in 1996; Elish's “moral crumple zone” (2019) showed how a token human absorbs blame for a system they could not control. Responsibility laundering names what agentic AI adds: design choices that make the gap routine rather than exceptional. Which choices matter — context-free assistant personas that speak from nowhere, synthesis that strips provenance and source status, permission models that bound what an agent can access but never what it is for, and the absence of a genuine contestation path? Even when a human approves the output, the agent has already shaped the frame in which the decision became visible.

2. *The capability-and-incentives pipeline.* How does a benchmark result become a sales deck, a procurement decision, and a deployment mandate, and where do critical evaluation and accountability fail? How do venture, vendor, and buyer incentives select frictionless assistants over bounded, contestable systems? The gap is documented cautiously: recent MIT and RAND analyses report widespread failure to produce measurable value from enterprise AI projects. This project follows the pipeline to the layer where those claims become requirements and defaults — and where failures can be absorbed or rebranded rather than reported.

3. *The buildable alternative — with receipts.* What would a situated agent require in order to act within an organization without laundering responsibility? My work revolves around *situated agents*: a named role, bounded scope, provenance-carrying output, explicit escalation, and a genuine contestation path. I am building this pattern as [open-source infrastructure](https://github.com/rizom-ai/brains) and running it with real organizations. The project will turn that practice into a published design standard precise enough for an engineer to implement and a procurement officer to require, while using the implementation to test whether the alternative works in the same situated environments where current defaults are produced.

**Why these questions are worth answering.** The public conversation about AI agents runs on vendor claims and lab announcements. The layer where those claims become decisions about real people — who gets flagged, prioritized, denied, or framed — is almost undocumented, because the people who see it are selling into it. Most near-term AI harm to ordinary people will be administered through this layer, not through frontier-model misbehavior.

Responsibility laundering is the diagnosis; situated agents are the alternative. Together they give journalists, regulators, and buyers handles they lack, while the reference implementation gives builders something to inspect, copy, and contest. The work in this layer provides unusual access and a clear situated interest. Therefore the evidence and implementation will run in the open: claims, design choices, deployment accounts, and code should be available for scrutiny rather than protected by the authority of insider status.

**Approach.** I will combine a technical anatomy of leading agent frameworks and enterprise assistant products with the practical access that comes from building and deploying these systems. I can inspect code, trace deployments, follow procurement conversations, and examine the design decisions that connect an agent's permissions, prompts, interfaces, provenance, and escalation paths to what it does in an organization. That access makes it possible to map each accountability gap to the technical and institutional choice that produces it, while engaging the accountability-gap literature in language legible to non-engineers without flattening the engineering.

The technical work will be placed alongside practitioner conversations and deployment accounts from engineers shipping agentic systems, implementation consultants, and buyers. These complements to code and deployment access will examine the incentives shaping defaults and the ways failures are absorbed or buried. They will not be organized around a predetermined interview quota. The project will culminate in a situated-agent design standard, published openly with a running open-source reference implementation: a named role, bounded scope, provenance-carrying output, explicit escalation, and a genuine contestation path. The standard will be precise enough for an engineer to implement and a procurement officer to require, and the implementation will test whether the proposed alternative works in the situated environments where current defaults are produced.

**Deliverables (6 months).** The central output will be a report, a situated-agent specification, and a running open-source reference implementation. Alongside these: 2–3 op-eds (e.g., “Your New AI Coworker Has No Job Description,” “Nobody Reports a Failed AI Project”) and a conversation on *Your Undivided Attention* or another CHT platform — the fellowship's podcast expectation — tracing how one agent-shaped decision travels through an organization and where answerability leaks out. All work happens as open-notebook research in a [public, provenance-tracked knowledge base](https://yeehaa.io/dashboard) running on the same open-source infrastructure the project examines.

## Fit

I am a former assistant professor at the University of Amsterdam turned founder, with a decade of building knowledge, learning, and coordination infrastructure for organizations, most recently at Rizom. I move between the engineering of these systems and the philosophical questions about cognition, authority, and responsibility they raise. I have watched institutional offloading happen from inside deployments, and I can translate between philosophy, empirical research, and what practitioners experience.

I am not a frontier-lab insider. I am an insider of the layer where frontier claims are implemented, monetized, and encountered by the public: the organizations, procurement processes, technical systems, and design decisions through which AI becomes consequential. That situated access is the basis for both proposals, while their questions remain distinct: one concerns institutional memory and judgment; the other concerns responsibility when AI acts.
