# CHT Fellowship Proposals

## Proposal 1: The Outsourced Institution

### What Happens When Organizations Offload Memory and Judgment to AI

**Summary.** In *Institutions That Can Carry Themselves* (2026), I describe a foundation whose fluent AI assistant could summarize every program and find every budget, yet could not explain why one project was renewed while another quietly disappeared. The institution had preserved everything except its reasons. CHT asks whether AI is more like a calculator or something qualitatively different for human thinking. I propose to answer that question at a level the current debate almost entirely ignores: the institution. Humans have never thought alone — we think through organizations, archives, roles, and procedures that remember, judge, and decide on our behalf. Institutions are now offloading their cognition to AI faster than any individual, and we have almost no rigorous account of what that does to collective judgment. This project extends the cognitive-offloading question from the individual mind to the institutional one, and asks: what happens to a society when its organizations can retrieve everything and remember nothing?

**Research questions.**

1. *Retrieval versus memory.* Institutional memory is not storage; it is the capacity to make the past available to present judgment — knowing which decisions are still alive, which documents are ceremonial, which exceptions became practice. When AI summarization and retrieval substitute for that capacity, what exactly is lost? Can the difference be made observable?

2. *Fluency versus judgment.* AI compresses weak institutional context into confident, fluent output. Under what conditions does that fluency displace the deliberation it imitates — and make institutional gaps harder to see and harder to contest?

3. *Cognitive monoculture.* CHT's description asks what it means for diversity of thought when hundreds of millions of people rely on the same small set of models. The institutional version is sharper: when thousands of organizations route their internal sense-making through identical foundation models, do their judgments converge? What are the systemic risks of correlated institutional cognition? How does this affect markets, public administration, and media?

4. *The disappearing apprenticeship.* Institutional judgment has historically been transmitted through the humans who carried it: veterans, fixers, coordinators. If AI absorbs their tasks without absorbing their context, how does the next generation of institutional judgment form at all? This is the adults-versus-kids question in organizational form: what happens to those who never practice the delegated task?

**Why these questions are worth answering.** The individual level now has real findings: search access reshapes what people internalize versus where they expect to find it (Sparrow, Liu & Wegner, *Science* 2011); heavier reliance on AI tools correlates with weaker critical-thinking performance (Gerlich 2025); knowledge workers report substantially less critical-thinking effort on AI-assisted tasks, especially when their confidence in the tool is high (Lee et al., CHI 2025); and EEG work on LLM-assisted writing finds reduced neural engagement and lower ownership of the result (Kosmyna et al. 2025). The institutional level has almost none of this, even though most consequential decisions — medical, financial, governmental, educational — are made by institutions, not individuals. 

My working hypothesis is that AI functions as an *institutional stress test*: as a transactive partner every member can query, it rewrites the group's directory of who-knows-what, exposing and amplifying cognitive decay rather than creating it, and compressing missing context into plausible output. If that is right, harms will surface not as dramatic model failures but as quiet erosion of institutions' capacity to explain, contest, and stand behind their own decisions. A mechanism for institutional distrust usually attributed to politics alone. The field currently runs on anecdote; distinguishing what we know from what we suspect is precisely the work.

**Alignment with CHT's framing.** These outcomes are not inevitable properties of the technology. They are predictable products of design choices: chat interfaces that present synthesis without provenance or status; business models: per-seat productivity tools optimized for output volume, not decision quality; and institutional incentives: throughput metrics that reward fast fluent answers over contestable ones. Naming the mechanism creates the possibility of different choices — interfaces that carry provenance, deployment norms that protect deliberation, procurement standards that ask what a tool does to judgment rather than only to throughput.

**Approach.** This will be a practice-based inquiry grounded in the concrete deployments and decisions through which institutions offload memory and judgment. I will trace what changes when an organization introduces AI into its knowledge work: what gets made retrievable, what gets summarized away, which exceptions remain visible, who is expected to remember the reasons, and how decisions are subsequently explained or contested. My position as a philosopher-builder matters here. I can examine these changes from inside the technical work — through the systems I build and deploy, the design choices they encode, and the institutional conversations around their adoption — while remaining attentive to the philosophical distinctions those changes put under pressure.

The inquiry will develop and test two distinctions in situated technical experiments: storage versus memory, and fluency versus judgment. These are not categories imposed in advance on organizations. They are working questions to be tested against real interfaces, records, workflows, and decisions. Open infrastructure will make the experiments inspectable and revisable. Where possible, I will work with organizations during the process of offloading, including deployments in which I have direct access, while balancing that perspective with cases outside my commercial relationships. The aim is not a predetermined sample or an artificial fixed interview count, but a body of concrete episodes rich enough to show where the distinctions hold, where they fail, and what institutional alternatives might look like.

Transactive memory theory (Wegner) and distributed cognition (Hutchins) will serve as interpretive frameworks rather than as a theory-led research design. Wegner's account helps explain how groups distribute knowledge and maintain a shared directory of who knows what; Hutchins situates institutional thinking across people, tools, and environments. Together they provide language for interpreting the deployments without dictating what they must reveal. The emerging empirical literature on cognitive offloading will be brought into conversation with the cases, with care about where individual-level findings do and do not transfer to institutions.

**Deliverables (6 months).** Flagship report *The Outsourced Institution* — framework, case evidence, and a practical diagnostic ("is this system extending our judgment or replacing it?"); 2–3 op-eds (e.g., "Your Company Doesn't Remember Anything Anymore," "The Coming Monoculture of Institutional Judgment"); a conversation on Your Undivided Attention or another CHT platform — the fellowship's podcast expectation — on why AI's real cognitive impact may run through our institutions rather than our brains. The research will be conducted as open-notebook research in a [public, provenance-tracked knowledge base](https://yeehaa.io/dashboard) — the kind of [infrastructure I build in my day job](https://github.com/rizom-ai/brains) — so any reader can trace every claim in the flagship report to its source; the method practices what the report argues.

**Fit.** Former assistant professor (University of Amsterdam) turned founder; a decade building knowledge and learning infrastructure for organizations, most recently at Rizom. I have watched the offloading happen from inside the deployments, and I can translate between the philosophy of cognition, the empirical literature, and what practitioners actually experience.


## Proposal 2: Responsibility Laundering

### How AI Agents Are Built to Act Without Answering — and the Open-Source Alternative

**Summary.** The industry is racing to deploy AI agents: systems that don't just answer questions but retrieve, draft, route, prioritize, and act inside organizations. 

I build these systems. I maintain an open-source agent framework and deploy it with real organizations. This gives me firsthand knowledge of the layer this focus area asks about: how agentic AI is actually wired together, how capability claims survive contact with a workplace, and what drives the design decisions that ship. 

From that seat, the central finding is this: today's agents are institutionally ambiguous because of deliberate design choices. They sound like analysts, act like coordinators, influence like managers, and disappear like tools. When something goes wrong there is no role that was exceeded, no provenance to inspect, no path for an affected person to contest the output. I call this *responsibility laundering*: agency passes through the machine and comes out clean. 

This is the companion to the Cognition proposal — where that asks what happens to institutional memory and judgment when they are outsourced to AI, this asks what happens to responsibility when action is delegated. The project pairs an insider's anatomy of how laundering happens with what critique alone cannot supply: a working, open-source proof that the accountable alternative is buildable. CHT asks what it would take to shift the incentives shaping this technology from the inside out. One answer: build the accountable version in the open, publish the standard, and give it away.

**Research questions.**

1. *The anatomy of responsibility laundering.* The accountability gap is not new — Nissenbaum named the "problem of many hands" and the "computer as scapegoat" in 1996; Elish's "moral crumple zone" (2019) showed how a token human absorbs blame for a system they could not control. Responsibility laundering names what agentic AI adds: design choices that make the gap routine rather than exceptional. Which choices? From practice: context-free assistant personas that speak from nowhere; synthesis that strips provenance and source status; permission models that bound what an agent can *access* but never what it is *for*; the missing contestation path. Even when a human approves the output, the agent has already shaped the frame in which the decision became visible — approval becomes the crumple zone.

2. *The capability-claims pipeline.* How does a benchmark result become a sales deck, a procurement decision, and a deployment mandate — and where does critical evaluation fail? The gap is now documented: MIT's 2025 *State of AI in Business* found 95% of enterprise generative-AI pilots deliver no measurable P&L impact; RAND reports over 80% of AI projects fail, roughly twice the rate of non-AI IT. This is CHT's question about evaluating capability claims, answered from the layer where the claims land: what a benchmark-topping agent actually does inside a municipality, a foundation, a company.

3. *The economics that select these defaults.* Accountability is friction, and friction loses procurement battles: general frictionless assistants demo better, sell faster, and deploy cheaper than bounded, contestable ones. And the failures above are rebranded rather than reported, so the "adopt or fall behind" narrative never meets its own base rates. This is the demand-side complement to the financial architecture CHT's description names — the venture and vendor incentives make sense only once you see what buyers are told and what failures are quietly absorbed.

4. *The buildable alternative — with receipts.* My work revolves around *situated agents*: named role, bounded scope, provenance-carrying output, explicit escalation and contestation paths. I am building this pattern as [open-source infrastructure](https://github.com/rizom-ai/brains) and running it with real organizations. This project turns that practice into a published design standard precise enough for an engineer to implement and a procurement officer to require — the existence proof that the current defaults are choices, not physics.

**Why these questions are worth answering.** The public conversation about AI agents runs on vendor claims and lab announcements. The layer where those claims become decisions about real people — who gets flagged, prioritized, denied, or framed — is almost undocumented, because the people who see it are selling into it. Most near-term AI harm to ordinary people will be administered through this layer, not through frontier-model misbehavior. 

Naming the mechanism — responsibility laundering, situated agents, ghost managers — gives journalists, regulators, and buyers handles they lack; a working reference implementation gives builders something to copy and buyers something to demand.

**Alignment with CHT's framing.** This is "harms are predictable products of business models and design choices" with a paper trail: this feature exists because it demos well; this accountability gap exists because closing it slows the sale. The constructive orientation is structural, not rhetorical. The deliverable is a standard plus running code, which is what "the trajectory is not inevitable" looks like when a technologist says it. Disclosure is part of the design: I sell in this layer, which is exactly why the analysis runs in the open where every claim can be checked. And I should be direct about positioning — I am not a frontier-lab insider. I am an insider of the layer where the frontier's claims are monetized and where the public's actual encounter with AI is decided; that is the technologist perspective this focus area most lacks.

**Approach.** I will combine a technical anatomy of leading agent frameworks and enterprise assistant products with the practical access that comes from building and deploying these systems. I can inspect code, trace deployments, and follow the design decisions that connect an agent's permissions, prompts, interfaces, provenance, and escalation paths to what it does in an organization. I can also observe the procurement conversations in which capability claims become requirements, and the implementation decisions through which those requirements become defaults. That access makes it possible to map each accountability gap to the technical and institutional choice that produces it, while engaging the accountability-gap literature (Nissenbaum, Elish) in language legible to non-engineers without dumbing down the engineering.

The technical work will be placed alongside conversations with engineers shipping agentic systems, implementation consultants, and buyers. These conversations will examine the incentives shaping defaults and the ways failures are absorbed or buried, without reducing the inquiry to a predetermined interview quota. The project will culminate in a situated-agent design standard, published openly with a running reference implementation: a named role, bounded scope, provenance-carrying output, explicit escalation, and a genuine contestation path. The standard is intended to be precise enough for an engineer to implement and a procurement officer to require, and the implementation will test whether the proposed alternative works in the same situated environments where the current defaults are produced.

**Deliverables (6 months).** Flagship report *Responsibility Laundering*, closing with a published situated-agent specification; 2–3 op-eds (e.g., "Your New AI Coworker Has No Job Description," "Nobody Reports a Failed AI Project"); a conversation on Your Undivided Attention or another CHT platform — the fellowship's podcast expectation — tracing how one agent-shaped decision travels through an organization and where answerability leaks out. All work happens as open-notebook research in a [public, provenance-tracked knowledge base](https://yeehaa.io/dashboard) running on the same open-source infrastructure the project examines — the research is itself a live demonstration of the design standard it proposes.

**Fit.** A decade building knowledge and coordination infrastructure for organizations, most recently at Rizom, where I develop and deploy the agent systems this proposal examines; before that, an assistant professor at the University of Amsterdam. I move between the engineering of these systems and the philosophical questions about authority and responsibility they raise.
