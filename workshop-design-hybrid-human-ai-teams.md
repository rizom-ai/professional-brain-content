# Workshop: Designing Hybrid Human-AI Teams

## Workshop Overview

**Title:** Who Knows What? Designing Hybrid Human-AI Teams That Actually Work
**Format:** Half-day (3.5 hours), adaptable for in-person or remote
**Audience:** Team leads and managers in professional/corporate settings (participants likely already use AI tools)
**Style:** Presentation and discussion-driven, practitioner-oriented with research grounding
**Goal:** Help leaders establish concrete workflows and protocols for human-AI collaboration

---

## Narrative Arc

The workshop follows a single throughline: **the problem isn't the AI — it's the team design.** Most AI implementations fail not because the technology is bad, but because teams don't know who knows what anymore. We use Transactive Memory Systems (TMS) and Shared Mental Models as the theoretical lens, then translate into actionable protocols.

---

## Session Breakdown

### Session 1: The Real Problem (45 min)
**Key message:** AI teams fail at the organizational level, not the technical level.

**Content:**
- Opening provocation: "We've been thinking about AI teams all wrong. We obsess over the AI — its training, its reasoning, its alignment — when the real problem is much older: *trust in who knows what*."
- What is a Transactive Memory System? The shared understanding of who knows what in a group. Not individual intelligence — collective knowledge architecture.
- The couple married 30 years analogy: they don't explain things; they know intuitively who remembers the directions, who knows the recipe. That's TMS.
- Why breaking up high-performing teams is catastrophic even if you keep the same people — you've destroyed the memory system.
- What happens when you add AI: "Does the AI understand context? Should I ask it or the human expert? Who's responsible if this goes wrong?" The team's TMS gets scrambled. Trust collapses.

**Discussion prompt:** Where have you seen knowledge "exist everywhere and nowhere simultaneously" in your teams?

**Sources:** TMS blog post, Offcourse/Lefthoek experience with distributed teams

---

### Session 2: Shared Mental Models Meet AI Agents (45 min)
**Key message:** TMS tells you *who knows what*. Shared Mental Models tell you *how we work together*. Both break when AI enters the picture.

**Content:**
- Shared Mental Models: the team's implicit understanding of how tasks get done, who plays what role, and how decisions flow
- TMS vs SMM: complementary frameworks
  - **TMS** = the knowledge directory ("Sarah understands customer psychology, the AI understands data patterns, I understand product constraints")
  - **SMM** = the playbook ("When we get a new data request, the AI runs the exploratory work, surfaces options, and the human brings judgment")
- The agentic shift: AI agents aren't tools you use — they're team members with genuinely alien expertise. They see patterns humans miss, but they also hallucinate confidently and lack context.
- The "agency trumps humanness" principle: What matters isn't whether a team member is human — it's whether they effectively contribute. Design for capability, not species.
- Calibrating trust in AI expertise: "The AI is good at synthesis and pattern-matching. It's terrible at stakes and consequences. When something matters, we verify with human judgment."

**Discussion prompt:** Map your current team's TMS. Where does AI already hold knowledge? Where is it unclear?

**Sources:** Agency Trumps Humanness essay, Bots with an Attitude project, TMS blog post

### Session 3: The Architecture of Trust (45 min)
**Key message:** Trust isn't magical. It's built on predictability. Design for it.

**Content:**
- Clarity about agency — not authority, but *agency*. Who decides? Who *can't* decide?
- The boundary problem: teams blur the line between "what the AI is good at" and "what the AI should do"
  - **AI excels at:** pattern recognition, generating options, catching inconsistencies, synthesis at scale
  - **Humans excel at:** navigating contested ambiguity, making consequential calls, understanding context, knowing when to break the rules
- Designing explicit role clarity:
  - Advisory vs. consequential decisions
  - "Suggest and surface" vs. autonomous action
  - The Offcourse lesson: the moment the system made autonomous decisions about people's educational futures, everything broke — not technically, socially
- The attitude problem: Design AI to be honest about limitations, not likeable. Systems that say "I'm uncertain" instead of hallucinating confidently. Character enables collaboration.
- Building the protocols:
  - Decision logs (not just what was decided, but *who decided and why*)
  - Expertise maps (living documents of who/what knows what)
  - Escalation boundaries (when does AI hand off to human?)
  - Feedback loops (how does the team learn and update its TMS?)

**Discussion prompt:** Pick one decision your team makes regularly. Who should own it — human, AI, or hybrid? What's the handoff protocol?

**Sources:** Designing Hybrid AI-Human Teams essay, Bots with an Attitude governance model, Lefthoek project

---

### Session 4: From Theory to Protocol (45 min)
**Key message:** Fix the memory system first. The AI will follow.

**Content:**
- **The Hybrid Team Protocol Canvas** — a takeaway framework with four quadrants:
  1. **Knowledge Directory** (TMS): Who knows what? Map human expertise, AI capabilities, and the gaps
  2. **Operating Playbook** (SMM): How do tasks flow? Define the handoffs between human and AI
  3. **Trust Boundaries**: What's advisory vs. consequential? Where does AI act autonomously vs. with human sign-off?
  4. **Learning Loops**: How does the team update its understanding? Decision logs, retrospectives, TMS reviews
- Walking through the canvas with a concrete example (e.g., a content team using AI agents for research, drafting, and publishing)
- Common failure modes (optional deep-dive):
  - Over-reliance / automation complacency
  - Deskilling — humans losing expertise they've delegated
  - Trust collapse — one bad AI output poisons the whole system
  - The "tool trap" — treating agents as tools instead of team members
- Practical next steps for participants:
  1. Audit your team's current TMS — where is knowledge implicit vs. explicit?
  2. Map your AI agents' actual capabilities (not marketing claims)
  3. Define three explicit trust boundaries this week
  4. Start a decision log

**Sources:** All of the above, synthesized into actionable framework

### Closing: A Living Example (15 min)
**Key message:** This isn't theoretical — it's being built right now.

**Content:**
- Brief introduction to Rizom Brains: personal knowledge systems where human and AI intelligence are co-constitutive from the start
- How Brains embody the principles from the workshop:
  - Full ownership (markdown + git, not vendor lock-in)
  - Distributed intelligence (personal brains + AI agents that understand context)
  - Open standards (HTTP, MCP, ACP — preventing walled gardens)
  - The AI learns *your* context, values, and frameworks — not generic patterns
- The philosophy: treating knowledge infrastructure as a living system, not a machine
- Q&A

**Sources:** Rizom product vision, "The Problem Is Real" document

## Key Takeaways for Participants

1. **TMS is the foundation** — before you optimize AI, map who knows what
2. **Agency trumps humanness** — design for capability, not species
3. **Trust = predictability** — make boundaries explicit, not implicit
4. **Shared Mental Models + TMS** — you need both the directory and the playbook
5. **Fix the memory system first** — the AI will follow
