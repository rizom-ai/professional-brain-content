---
title: Institutional Decay
---
This topic examines how institutions can appear intact while quietly losing their authority, memory, and coordinating power. Formal structures remain in place, but their substance hollows out as decisions shift offstage and procedures become ceremonial. It treats this as a structural problem rather than a personal one: visible continuity can mask deep functional collapse. A key sign of decay is when organizations increasingly depend on exceptional people or informal workarounds to do work the institution itself no longer carries. In this view, heroics are often a symptom of institutional failure, not a sign of health, and decay is understood as a gradual erosion of form rather than an abrupt collapse of the whole. The topic is useful for analyzing organizational legitimacy, coordination failure, and the gap between official process and actual practice in institutions, nonprofits, governments, and large enterprises often needing workarounds, informal coordination, or exceptional labor to function. It emphasizes authority, institutional memory, procedural hollowing, and hidden breakdowns in organizational systems. It is relevant when examining bureaucracy, governance, organizational resilience, and systemic dysfunction across formal organizations and public institutions.
