---
title: Digital Ecosystem Fragmentation
---
The internet is presented as a fragmented collection of regional, technical, governmental, and commercial ecosystems rather than a single unified global network. This fragmentation reflects different technological histories, geopolitical conditions, economic models, and cultural contexts. Examples include Asian superapps, African mesh networks, national internet systems, mobile app stores, and the earlier divisions between ARPANET, European academic networks, and France’s Minitel. Although fragmentation can produce barriers, surveillance, isolation, and interoperability problems, it can also support localized innovation, privacy, data sovereignty, cultural preservation, and resilience. The article challenges efforts to restore an idealized open global internet, arguing that this historical model was exceptional and unable to produce universal trust or consensus. Ecosystem architecture therefore treats fragmentation as a condition to understand and cultivate rather than a defect to eliminate.
