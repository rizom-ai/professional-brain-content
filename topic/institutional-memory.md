---
title: Institutional Memory
---
Institutional memory is the capacity to make an organization’s past meaningful for present judgment, not merely to store documents or retrieve information. It preserves context, provenance, exceptions, and the reasons behind earlier decisions, connecting records to practices so people can understand why those decisions still matter. Without usable and contestable memory, fragmented archives shift interpretive power toward veterans, fixers, and insiders who know how to reconstruct undocumented history. Digital tools, including AI retrieval and summarization, can improve access but may also conceal missing context behind fluent outputs. Durable institutional memory therefore depends on linked, contextual, revisable representations, collective interpretation, and deliberate pruning of obsolete records and assumptions. It is also political: archival structures shape visibility, participation, authority, organizational learning, and what an institution considers meaningful.

Keywords: institutional memory, knowledge management, organizational archives, organizational learning, information retrieval, provenance, collective judgment, contextual knowledge, decision records, deliberate forgetting
