---
title: Humanities Tool Design
---
The discussion of Coding the Humanities argues that digital tools shape research and teaching practices but are rarely treated as objects of scholarly reflection. Generic word processors lack semantic markup, citation management, and meaningful collaboration features for humanities research. Online teaching platforms often reproduce classroom metaphors instead of using technology to transform how teaching and learning occur. Existing tools also reinforce the separation between research and education, despite the close relationship between those activities. In contrast, software developers deliberately shape their tools through experimentation, customization, community debate, and open source development. Appropriate tools do more than improve efficiency: they help practitioners distinguish different kinds of problems and discover new possibilities. The central claim is that deliberate tool use is a condition of craftsmanship and can drive an entire field forward.
