---
title: Humanities Tool Design
---
Humanities Tool Design concerns the deliberate creation of digital tools that fit the actual practices of research and teaching. The content argues that generic word processors and classroom-mimicking learning platforms are poorly suited to scholarly work because they lack semantic markup, citation management, meaningful collaboration, and integration between research and education. Humanities scholars often focus on content while ignoring the structures and tools that produce knowledge. Software developers, by contrast, actively shape their environments through customization, experimentation, community discussion, and tool creation. Appropriate tools do more than improve efficiency: they help practitioners distinguish different kinds of problems and discover new possibilities. Tool design is therefore presented as a central part of craftsmanship and disciplinary development. Coding the Humanities emerges from the conviction that practice and infrastructure deserve as much attention as scholarly output.
