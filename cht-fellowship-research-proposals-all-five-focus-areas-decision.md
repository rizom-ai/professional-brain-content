## Proposal 1 — Focus Area: AI and Cognition

### The Outsourced Institution: What Happens When Organizations Offload Memory and Judgment to AI

**Summary.** CHT asks whether AI is more like a calculator or something qualitatively different for human thinking. I propose to answer that question at a level the current debate almost entirely ignores: the institution. Humans have never thought alone — we think through organizations, archives, roles, and procedures that remember, judge, and decide on our behalf. Institutions are now offloading their cognition to AI faster than any individual, and we have almost no rigorous account of what that does to collective judgment. This project extends the cognitive-offloading question from the individual mind to the institutional one, and asks: what happens to a society when its organizations can retrieve everything and remember nothing?

**Research questions.**

1. *Retrieval versus memory.* Institutional memory is not storage; it is the capacity to make the past available to present judgment — knowing which decisions are still alive, which documents are ceremonial, which exceptions became practice. When AI summarization and retrieval substitute for that capacity, what exactly is lost? Can the difference be made observable?
2. *Fluency versus judgment.* AI compresses weak institutional context into confident, fluent output. Under what conditions does that fluency displace the deliberation it imitates — and make institutional gaps harder to see and harder to contest?
3. *Cognitive monoculture.* CHT's description asks what it means for diversity of thought when hundreds of millions of people rely on the same small set of models. The institutional version is sharper: when thousands of organizations route their internal sense-making through identical foundation models, do their judgments converge? What are the systemic risks of correlated institutional cognition — in markets, public administration, media?
4. *The disappearing apprenticeship.* Institutional judgment has historically been transmitted through the humans who carried it: veterans, fixers, coordinators. If AI absorbs their tasks without absorbing their context, how does the next generation of institutional judgment form at all? (This is the adults-versus-kids question in organizational form: what happens to those who never practice the delegated task?)

**Why these questions are worth answering.** The individual-cognition debate has an emerging evidence base; the institutional one has almost none, even though most consequential decisions — medical, financial, governmental, educational — are made by institutions, not individuals. My working hypothesis, developed in the white paper, is that AI functions as an *institutional stress test*: it does not create cognitive decay in organizations, it exposes and amplifies it, compressing missing context into plausible output. If that is right, harms will surface not as dramatic model failures but as quiet erosion of institutions' capacity to explain, contest, and stand behind their own decisions — a mechanism for institutional distrust usually attributed to politics alone. The field currently runs on anecdote; distinguishing what we know from what we suspect is precisely the work.

**Alignment with CHT's framing.** These outcomes are not inevitable properties of the technology. They are predictable products of design choices (chat interfaces that present synthesis without provenance or status), business models (per-seat productivity tools optimized for output volume, not decision quality), and institutional incentives (throughput metrics that reward fast fluent answers over contestable ones). Naming the mechanism creates the possibility of different choices — interfaces that carry provenance, deployment norms that protect deliberation, procurement standards that ask what a tool does to judgment rather than only to throughput.

**Approach.** (a) A conceptual framework drawing on distributed and extended cognition (Hutchins; Clark and Chalmers), organizational memory research, and my background as a philosopher — establishing precise, usable distinctions between storage/memory and fluency/judgment. (b) Qualitative case studies: structured interviews with practitioners in 6–10 organizations that have deployed AI assistants over internal knowledge (a public agency, a foundation, a mid-size company, a newsroom), tracing concrete decisions before and after adoption. (c) A synthesis of the emerging empirical literature on cognitive offloading, honestly marking where individual-level findings do and do not transfer to institutions.

**Deliverables (6 months).** Flagship report *The Outsourced Institution* — framework, case evidence, and a practical diagnostic ("is this system extending our judgment or replacing it?"); 2–3 op-eds (e.g., "Your Company Doesn't Remember Anything Anymore," "The Coming Monoculture of Institutional Judgment"); a podcast episode on why AI's real cognitive impact may run through our institutions rather than our brains.

**Fit.** Former assistant professor (University of Amsterdam) turned founder; a decade building knowledge and learning infrastructure for organizations, most recently at Rizom. I have watched the offloading happen from inside the deployments, and I can translate between the philosophy of cognition, the empirical literature, and what practitioners actually experience.

## Proposal 2 — Focus Area: AI and Relationships

### Relationships Without Repair: What Frictionless Machines Unlearn in Us

**Summary.** CHT's framing identifies the deep question precisely: not whether AI companions comfort people, but whether sustained interaction with something that "never judges, never tires, and never requires repair" recalibrates what people expect from one another. This project puts a name on the mechanism: the removal of *repair*. Empathy, trust, and intimacy are not built by smooth interaction; they are built by rupture and repair — misunderstanding someone and finding a way back. Companion AI is engineered to make repair unnecessary, and engagement-driven business models guarantee it stays that way: a product that risks rupture risks churn. I propose to study what happens to human relational capacities — individual and collective — when the repair loop is designed out.

**Research questions.**

1. *Repair as the developmental engine.* Developmental psychology and attachment research suggest relational capacities are built through cycles of rupture and repair, friction and reconciliation. What does that literature actually establish, and what does it predict about sustained interaction with partners incapable of genuine rupture?
2. *Frictionlessness as a business requirement.* Which specific, nameable design choices in companion and social-coach products (sycophancy tuning, infinite availability, memory without grudge, conflict-avoidant personas) remove repair — and how do retention economics make those choices nearly obligatory? This is CHT's core claim made concrete: the harm profile is a predictable product of the business model, not an accident.
3. *The collective repair capacity.* Relationships also hold communities together, and communities need repair most of all: conflict resolution, feedback, reconciliation, renegotiated trust. As AI increasingly mediates group coordination — drafting the difficult message, summarizing the dispute, smoothing the tone — do communities lose practice in exactly the capacities that let them survive conflict? My white paper documents how organizations already outsource frictional coordination to informal human "fixers"; AI is now absorbing that role at scale.
4. *What regenerative relational technology would require.* If frictionlessness is the pathology, the answer is not "worse products." Where does friction belong by design? What would companion or coordination tools look like if they were built to strengthen users' human relationships rather than substitute for them — and under what business model could such tools survive?

**Why these questions are worth answering.** The public debate oscillates between moral panic and anecdotes of comfort. The recalibration question CHT poses is the right one, and it is empirically tractable if someone does the work of connecting the developmental literature to actual product design. The collective dimension is almost untouched: families, congregations, workplaces, and civic groups are relational infrastructures, and their capacity for repair is what makes shared life possible. A society whose members increasingly practice relationship on entities that cannot rupture may keep the appearance of connection while losing the capability that makes connection durable.

**Alignment with CHT's framing.** This is the attention-economy critique carried into the intimacy economy: engagement optimization applied not to content but to companionship. And it is constructive: by locating the harm in specific design choices rather than in "AI" as such, it points at concrete alternatives — design norms, disclosure standards, and evaluation criteria ("does this product increase or decrease the user's human relational capacity?") that regulators, builders, and buyers can use.

**Approach.** (a) A rigorous synthesis of rupture-and-repair research (attachment, developmental psychology, conflict studies), honestly marking what transfers to human–AI interaction and what remains open. (b) A design audit of leading companion and social-coach products, mapping features to the repair loop they remove and to the retention metrics that motivate them. (c) Practitioner and user interviews, including community leaders (educators, congregational leaders, team leads) on how AI mediation is changing conflict and reconciliation in their groups — drawing on my decade of building coordination tools for distributed communities.

**Deliverables (6 months).** Flagship essay/report *Relationships Without Repair*; 2–3 op-eds (e.g., "The Best Friend Who Never Fights Back," "AI Is Making Us Worse at Making Up"); a podcast episode on repair as the hidden infrastructure of human connection.

## Proposal 3 — Focus Area: AI and Surveillance

### Total Recall: How Workplace AI Is Building Surveillance Infrastructure by Accident

**Summary.** CHT's description notes that AI products designed for productivity are "simultaneously fueling intimate surveillance infrastructure." I propose to document the clearest case: the AI-powered workplace. To make AI assistants useful, organizations are recording everything — every meeting transcribed, every chat retained, every document embedded, every decision logged. Each step is justified as memory: the institution finally remembering what it knows. But memory infrastructure and surveillance infrastructure are made of identical parts — capture, storage, inference, retrieval. What distinguishes them is not the technology but the governance: who can query it, for what purpose, with what visibility, and with what right to contest. Almost no organization deploying these systems can answer those questions. This project maps the build-out, names the inflection point where memory becomes surveillance, and specifies the protections — legal and technical — that would keep the archive answerable to the people inside it.

**Research questions.**

1. *The capture build-out.* What are enterprise AI products actually recording, retaining, and inferring? A systematic audit of the deployment layer — meeting assistants, copilots, "employee insight" analytics — documenting defaults, retention, and the inference features (sentiment, engagement, productivity scoring) arriving one product update at a time.
2. *The memory/surveillance boundary.* Under what governance conditions does the same trace serve memory rather than monitoring? The white paper's answer: purpose-binding, provenance, access transparency, contestation rights, and deletion. Can that boundary be specified sharply enough for procurement standards, works-council agreements, and regulation?
3. *Diffuse harm and labor power.* CHT asks how to think about harm that is invisible to the person experiencing it. The workplace shows the mechanism at its clearest: chilling effects on candor and dissent, inference asymmetry (the employer's model knows things the worker never disclosed), and the quiet conversion of trust into telemetry — a direct question of labor power, as CHT's framing names it.
4. *What meaningful protection requires.* Which existing instruments (GDPR purpose limitation, European works-council codetermination, emerging AI acts) actually reach AI-era workplace inference, and where are the gaps? What technical architectures — data sovereignty, worker-visible audit trails, purpose-bound access — make protection real rather than policy-theater?

**Why these questions are worth answering.** Surveillance debates focus on states and consumer platforms; the workplace is where most adults will first live inside an AI inference system, for eight hours a day, with the least ability to opt out. And the infrastructure is being built by accident — procured as productivity, experienced as monitoring — which means the window for setting norms is now, before the defaults harden. There is also a genuine dilemma here that makes the work intellectually honest rather than alarmist: organizations really do need memory (my white paper argues exactly that), so the answer cannot be "record nothing." The question is what makes total recall governable.

**Alignment with CHT's framing.** The surveillance is not a conspiracy; it is a predictable product of incentives. Vendors add inference features because "insight" dashboards sell; employers adopt them because monitoring is framed as duty-of-care and productivity; workers cannot refuse because the tools are the workflow. Naming that chain creates leverage at each link — and the constructive orientation is built in, because the same analysis that exposes the surveillance specifies the governance that would make institutional memory legitimate.

**Approach.** (a) Product audit of leading workplace AI tools (documentation, DPAs, admin consoles, changelogs). (b) Interviews with workers, works-council members, CISOs, and vendors — I bring a European vantage point, where codetermination and GDPR create both real protections and instructive failures. (c) Legal-technical gap analysis with privacy and labor scholars, distinguishing what current law reaches from what it misses. My qualification is unusual here: I build knowledge infrastructure for organizations at Rizom, which means I know exactly how these systems are architected — and how they could be architected differently.

**Deliverables (6 months).** Flagship report *Total Recall* with a governance framework separating memory from surveillance; 2–3 op-eds (e.g., "Your Meeting Assistant Is a Microphone," "The Right to Be Forgotten by Your Employer"); a podcast episode on the AI workplace as the front line of surveillance normalization.

## Proposal 4 — Focus Area: AI and Spirituality

### Oracles Without Community: AI and the Institutions That Carry Meaning

**Summary.** CHT's framing identifies two entangled phenomena: AI products moving into the spaces spiritual traditions have occupied, and an AI industry saturated with its own theology of salvation and transcendence. This project connects them through a single question: what happens to meaning when it is detached from the institutions that carry it? Spiritual guidance has never been merely answers. A pastor, rabbi, imam, or teacher is a *situated* authority — holding a named role inside a tradition, formed by transmission, accountable to a community, bound by obligations the seeker can see. A chatbot offering moral guidance is an *unsituated* authority: fluent answers from nowhere, formed by training data and engagement incentives, accountable to no congregation. The comparison is not nostalgia; it is an analytical instrument for understanding what the commodification of meaning-making actually removes — and it applies with equal force to Silicon Valley's own godless theology, which claims transcendence while refusing every institutional structure (doctrine open to contest, community accountability, obligations of care) that made religious authority answerable.

**Research questions.**

1. *What spiritual institutions actually do.* Across traditions, meaning is carried by living institutions: ritual, transmission, situated authority, communal repair. Drawing on philosophy of religion and comparative cases, what are the functional components of institutional meaning-making — and which of them can, and cannot, survive translation into a consumer product?
2. *The engagement-optimized oracle.* Spiritual-guidance apps and general chatbots used as confessors answer existential questions under retention incentives. What do these products actually optimize for, and how does optimizing meaning for engagement differ from rooting it in wisdom, tradition, or community — CHT's question made empirically tractable through product analysis?
3. *Authority without accountability.* When a person accepts moral guidance from an AI, what structure of authority are they entering? Who formed it, who can contest it, who is responsible for its counsel? The white paper calls this pattern "speaking from nowhere" in institutional AI; here it reaches its most consequential form.
4. *Silicon Valley's theology as institutional strategy.* Scholars have begun mapping the salvation-and-transcendence rhetoric as coherent ideology. The unexamined angle is institutional: this theology performs religion's legitimating functions (ultimate purpose, eschatology, chosen-ness) while systematically avoiding religion's accountability structures. What work does that asymmetry do in how AI development is justified to the public?

**Why these questions are worth answering.** The meaning-making functions of religious institutions are migrating to products regardless of what anyone thinks about it — the pastoral-AI market is growing fastest precisely where institutional religion is weakest. If the analysis stops at "people are praying to chatbots," it produces alarm without understanding. The institutional lens explains *what specifically is lost* (accountability, transmission, communal repair), *why the substitution is attractive* (institutions of meaning have themselves stopped carrying — my white paper's central diagnosis applied to its hardest case), and *what the industry's own theology is doing* (borrowing transcendence while refusing answerability). That gives journalists, faith leaders, and policymakers a vocabulary more useful than either panic or dismissal.

**Alignment with CHT's framing.** Commodified meaning-making is a predictable product of engagement economics entering a domain with no professional norms, no clergy confidentiality, no heresy trials — no accountability structure of any kind. And the trajectory is not inevitable: the analysis yields concrete distinctions (guidance vs. companionship claims, disclosure of optimization targets, referral duties at crisis moments) that builders and regulators can act on, and it equips faith communities to make deliberate rather than accidental choices about AI in their own practice.

**Approach.** (a) Conceptual framework from philosophy and institutional theory — my formation is as a philosopher in the continental tradition, where the relationship between meaning, authority, and institution is a central inheritance. (b) Product and discourse analysis: spiritual-guidance apps, chatbot pastoral use, and the public theology of AI leadership (essays, manifestos, podcast statements), read with the same analytical seriousness scholars give religious texts. (c) Comparative interviews with faith leaders across traditions on what AI can and cannot hold in their practice — conducted with the cross-cultural care CHT's description asks for, and with honesty about where my competence ends and religious-studies scholarship must be brought in.

**Deliverables (6 months).** Flagship essay *Oracles Without Community*; 2–3 op-eds (e.g., "The Chatbot Will Hear Your Confession Now," "Silicon Valley Built a Religion With No Way to Say No to God"); a podcast episode on why meaning has always needed institutions, and what happens when it gets an API instead.

## Proposal 5 — Focus Area: Inside the Machine: AI From a Technologist's Perspective

### Where Capability Claims Meet Reality: A Builder's Account of the AI Deployment Layer

**Summary.** CHT seeks insiders who know how AI systems are built and how capability claims should be critically evaluated. I offer insider knowledge of a layer the frontier-lab discourse skips: the deployment layer, where AI is sold into and embedded inside real organizations. I have spent a decade building knowledge infrastructure for institutions — learning platforms, credentialing systems, and now Rizom — which means I sit where the capability claims land: where "agents that transform your organization" meets an actual municipality, foundation, or company. From that seat, two things are visible that neither lab insiders nor outside critics can see well. First, how capability claims degrade between the benchmark and the workplace — and why the gap is systematically hidden by the incentives of vendors, consultants, and the executives who signed the purchase order. Second, how agentic systems are actually being wired into organizational decision-making: with what permissions, what oversight, and — the finding that motivates this proposal — what near-total absence of answerability. I call the resulting pattern *responsibility laundering*: agency passes through the machine and comes out clean.

**Research questions.**

1. *The capability-claims pipeline.* How does a benchmark result become a sales deck, a procurement decision, and a deployment mandate? Where exactly does critical evaluation fail — and what would honest, deployment-grade capability evaluation look like? This is the practical version of CHT's question about how capability claims should be critically evaluated.
2. *The economics of the deployment gap.* Enterprise AI pricing, consultancy incentives, and sunk-cost dynamics mean failed deployments are rebranded rather than reported. What can be documented about the real success base rates that the public narrative — "adopt or fall behind" — conceals? (This is the demand-side complement to the circular-deal financial analysis CHT's description names: the spending only defies business logic until you see what buyers are told.)
3. *Anatomy of responsibility laundering.* Which specific design choices in agent architectures erase answerability — context-free assistant personas, synthesis that strips provenance, permission models that bound what an agent can access but not what it is for, absent contestation paths? Even when a human approves the output, the agent has already shaped the frame in which the decision became visible.
4. *The buildable alternative.* The white paper specifies *situated agents* — named role, bounded scope, provenance-carrying output, explicit escalation and contestation paths. This project turns that into a published design standard precise enough for an engineer to implement and a procurement officer to require. I am building versions of it at Rizom; the trajectory is not inevitable, because the alternative already runs.

**Why these questions are worth answering.** The public conversation about AI in organizations is conducted almost entirely in vendor claims and lab announcements. The layer where those claims become decisions about real people — who gets flagged, prioritized, denied, or framed — is undocumented, because the people who see it are selling into it. Most near-term AI harm to ordinary people will be administered through this layer, not through frontier-model misbehavior. Opening it to public understanding is exactly the kind of insider translation this focus area exists for.

**Alignment with CHT's framing.** The deployment layer is where "harms as predictable products of business models and design choices" stops being a thesis and becomes a paper trail: this feature exists because it demos well; this accountability gap exists because closing it slows the sale. And the orientation is constructive throughout — the deliverable is not only an exposé but a standard, giving builders, buyers, and regulators something to adopt, demand, or require.

**Approach.** (a) Technical anatomy of leading agent frameworks and enterprise assistant products, mapping each accountability gap to the design decision that produces it — written to be legible to non-engineers without dumbing down the engineering. (b) Interviews with the deployment ecosystem: engineers shipping agentic systems, implementation consultants, and buyers, on the incentives that shape defaults and bury failures. (c) The situated-agent specification, published openly with reference examples. I should be direct about positioning: I am not a frontier-lab insider. I am an insider of the layer where the frontier's claims are monetized — and that layer is where the public's encounter with AI is actually decided.

**Deliverables (6 months).** Flagship report *Responsibility Laundering*; 2–3 op-eds (e.g., "Your New AI Coworker Has No Job Description," "Nobody Reports a Failed AI Project"); a podcast episode tracing how one agent-shaped decision travels through an organization and where answerability leaks out.
