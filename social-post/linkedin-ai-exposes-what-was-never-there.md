Nobody did anything wrong. Someone was trying to help.

Your team works the same way. Years of intuition passing as a system. It runs—until someone new shows up and suddenly nobody knows who knows what anymore.

AI is that someone.

Not because it breaks anything. Because it exposes what was never there.

In a kitchen during service, the mise en place gets moved. A cook sends out the wrong sauce. The pass backs up. No malice. Just the friction of unspoken knowledge, tribal memory, invisible dependencies.

Most teams run on this. Transactive memory—who knows what, who to ask, what lives where. It's efficient until it isn't.

When you introduce AI, you're introducing a participant who doesn't have access to that invisible system. Suddenly you have to make it explicit. And that's when you realize: there was no system. There was just luck and good people.

I'm building a workshop called **86'd** to help teams see this clearly—and build something that actually scales.

The goal isn't to blame anyone. It's to understand what you've actually built, so you can intentionally build what comes next.
