AI adoption is too small a frame.

What enters an organization is not “a tool” in isolation.
It is a new participant in an existing institution.

And institutions are never neutral workflows.
They are memory, roles, habits, exceptions, responsibilities, conflicts, trust, and the quiet informal repairs people make every day to keep things working.

That is why so many AI projects stall in the same place.
The model may be capable.
The pilot may look clean.
But the surrounding institution is doing far more than the demo ever shows.

Who is allowed to trust the output?
Who corrects it when it is wrong?
Who carries the risk when it is wrong in a way that looks plausible?
Which exceptions does it understand, and which ones does it simply erase?

These are not technical edge cases.
They are organizational reality.

I wrote more about this shift in thinking here:
https://yeehaa.io/essays/a-colleague-without-context

The interesting question is no longer whether AI can automate a task.
It is how AI changes the institution that surrounds the task.

That is a very different design problem.

How are you thinking about AI in your organization: as workflow automation, or as something that enters the social fabric too?
