New media often fail because we treat them as damaged versions of old ones.

Online learning becomes “the lecture, but through a webcam.”
Remote work becomes “the office, but with more meetings.”
AI becomes “the assistant, but faster.”

That usually misses the point.

A new medium is not interesting because it can imitate the old one.
It is interesting because it makes different things possible.

Back in 2013, when I was thinking a lot about online education, the most exciting examples were not trying to recreate the classroom.

They had short lessons.
Interactive assignments.
Instant feedback.
Playful themes.
A rhythm that belonged to the medium itself.

Online was not a compromise.
It was a design space.

I think that lesson still matters.

Whenever a technology becomes mainstream, the first impulse is usually substitution:
How do we use this to do what we already do?

The more interesting question comes later:
What becomes possible now that was awkward, expensive, or impossible before?

That shift matters for education, remote work, collaboration, and AI.

The future rarely arrives as a better copy of the past.
It arrives when we stop forcing new tools to pretend they are old rooms.
