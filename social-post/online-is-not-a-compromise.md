New media often fail because we treat them as damaged versions of old ones. Online learning gets reduced to a lecture through a webcam. Remote work gets reduced to the office with more meetings. AI gets reduced to an assistant that happens to be faster.

That usually misses the point. A new medium is not interesting because it can imitate the old one more efficiently. It is interesting because it changes what can be designed in the first place.

Back in 2013, when I was thinking a lot about online education, the most exciting examples were not trying to recreate the classroom. They had short lessons, interactive assignments, instant feedback, playful themes, and a rhythm that belonged to the medium itself. Online was not a compromise. It was a design space.

I think that lesson still matters. Whenever a technology becomes mainstream, the first impulse is usually substitution: how do we use this to do what we already do? The more interesting question comes later: what becomes possible now that was awkward, expensive, or impossible before?

That shift matters for education, remote work, collaboration, and AI. The future rarely arrives as a better copy of the past. It arrives when we stop forcing new tools to pretend they are old rooms.
