Modern institutions rarely suffer from a lack of information.

More often, they suffer from a surplus of traces that no longer add up to memory.

The shared drive is full. The archive exists. The minutes were taken. The dashboard updates itself. The chat history goes back for years.

Everything is stored somewhere.

And still, people cannot find what they need in the way they need it.

That is because storage is not memory.

Storage keeps traces. Memory preserves orientation.

A record can show that a decision was made without preserving why it mattered. A dashboard can make an institution feel informed while quietly narrowing what it is able to notice. An archive can contain everything and still fail to help anyone understand what should be done next.

When the record is unusable, power belongs to those who can interpret the ruins.

The person who remembers old decisions becomes more important than the place where decisions supposedly live. The person who knows which document can be ignored becomes more important than the document. The person who understands the informal exception becomes more important than the formal rule.

This is where institutional memory becomes political.

New institutions do not simply need more documentation, more dashboards, or more capture. They need forms of memory close enough to practice to stay alive, but durable enough to travel across time.

They also need to learn what to let die.
