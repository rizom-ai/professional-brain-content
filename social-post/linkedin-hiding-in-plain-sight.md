Modern institutions rarely suffer from a lack of information. More often, they suffer from a surplus of traces that no longer add up to memory.

The shared drive is full, the archive exists, the minutes were taken, the dashboard updates itself, and the chat history goes back for years. Everything is stored somewhere, and still people cannot find what they need in the way they need it.

That is because storage is not memory. Storage keeps traces. Memory preserves orientation.

When the record is unusable, power belongs to those who can interpret the ruins: the person who remembers old decisions, knows which documents can be ignored, or understands the informal exception. This is where institutional memory becomes political.

New institutions do not simply need more documentation, more dashboards, or more capture. They need forms of memory close enough to practice to stay alive, but durable enough to travel across time.

I published the third essay in my New Institutions series: **Hiding in Plain Sight**.

https://yeehaa.io/essays/hiding-in-plain-sight

If you work in an organization where everything is documented but nothing is really findable, I’d be curious to hear how you deal with that gap.
