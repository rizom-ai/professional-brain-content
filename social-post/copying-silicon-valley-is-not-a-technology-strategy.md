title: Copying Silicon Valley Is Not A Technology Strategy

Copying Silicon Valley is not a technology strategy.

That is especially true for Europe and other middle powers. We do not have the same capital markets, platform monopolies, risk culture, or appetite for moving fast and externalizing the damage. This is often treated as a weakness, but it may also be the beginning of a more interesting innovation model.

The question is whether we can stop measuring technological ambition only through scale, speed, and capture. Much of the dominant innovation story assumes that the goal is to grow as fast as possible, centralize as much as possible, and turn every new layer of infrastructure into a platform. But that model does not travel everywhere, and perhaps it should not.

A different institutional imagination would start from different constraints: sovereignty over dependency, interoperability over lock-in, regeneration over extraction, distributed intelligence over platform centralization, accountable agents over opaque automation. These are not softer ambitions. They are harder ones, because they require technology to remain answerable to the institutions, communities, and ecologies it acts within.

That means data infrastructure that keeps control closer to the people and organizations that produce it; knowledge systems that make memory, context, and coordination portable without making them contextless; agents that act as situated collaborators rather than black-box automation layers; and community infrastructure organized around trust, learning, and shared action instead of centralized attention markets.

The opportunity is not to build another empire of apps with fewer resources. It is to build civic, organizational, and ecological infrastructure for a world that needs more than growth at all costs.

That is not a retreat from technological ambition. It is a better definition of it.
