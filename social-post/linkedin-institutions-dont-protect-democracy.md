The constitution was never really a safeguard.

It was a suspension of disbelief.

COVID showed us this. People willing to set aside constitutional protections for marginal gains in safety. Not because they were evil—because the social contract underneath those institutions had already fractured.

This is the uncomfortable truth we've collectively ignored: institutions only hold when there's alignment on shared values. When that contract breaks, the constitution becomes theater. Israel ignoring UN resolutions. Governments dismissing courts. Institutions recognized as legitimate only when they serve particular interests.

The left blames the right. The right blames the left. But the real problem is deeper: we've been trying to repair institutions when we should be rebuilding the social contracts beneath them.

The good news? We now have tools the industrial age never had. Decentralized alternatives. Asynchronous coordination. Ways to build civil societies that actually reflect how we work today.

But technology alone won't save us. Underneath the infrastructure must be something solid: aligned values, constantly debated and negotiated. A functioning social contract that balances individual rights with shared responsibilities.

This is what I'm exploring in a new series—how to rebuild institutions for an era where the old ones have lost their grip.
