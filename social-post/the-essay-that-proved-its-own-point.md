Today, an essay I wrote about institutional memory disappeared from my website.

The essay was called *Hiding in Plain Sight*.

Honestly, I could not have designed a better demonstration of its argument.

The essay had been published, indexed, and shared. Its content still existed. So did its Git history, images, links, and generated pages.

But one durable record still called it a draft.

After a deployment rollback and a full synchronization, the system did exactly what it had been designed to do: it trusted that stale record and removed the essay from production.

Nothing was lost. Everything was stored. Yet the institution forgot.

That is the distinction at the heart of the essay. Storage preserves traces. Memory preserves orientation: the context needed to understand what happened, what still matters, and what should happen next.

We like to call something a “source of truth,” as if authority guarantees accuracy. But a source of truth can still be wrong. It merely decides which version of reality wins when the system reconciles itself.

Sometimes an institution does not forget because its records are missing.

Sometimes it remembers the wrong thing with perfect confidence.
