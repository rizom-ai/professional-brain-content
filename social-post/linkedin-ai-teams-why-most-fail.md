Most AI implementations fail not because the technology isn't good enough.

They fail because we're trying to integrate something fundamentally new using frameworks built for the old.

We treat AI as a tool to optimize existing workflows. But AI isn't a hammer—it's a collaborator with different strengths, blindspots, and modes of thinking.

The real question isn't "how do we automate this?" It's "how do we structure a team where human judgment and AI capability actually complement each other?"

This means rethinking agency, accountability, and knowledge flow. It means understanding what humans bring that AI can't—context, values, intuition—and what AI brings that humans struggle with at scale.

I've been exploring this through the lens of transactive memory systems: how teams remember, decide, and act together. Most AI teams fail because they skip the hard work of designing these systems intentionally.

The ones that work? They're explicit about who decides what, how information flows, and where human oversight matters most.

This isn't about limiting AI. It's about amplifying human capacity by building teams that actually leverage what both can do.
