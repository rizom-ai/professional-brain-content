We're stuck between two fantasies about AI agents.

One: build them as obedient tools. Control everything.

Two: give them full autonomy and hope for the best.

Both miss what actually works.

The real insight? Agents aren't tools or overlords. They're participants in shared ecosystems of knowledge and trust.

I've been exploring this for years—from Planet B's distributed community agents to what we're building now with Rizom. The pattern is always the same: intelligence doesn't emerge from design. It emerges from interaction.

This is why "Have Your Agent Call My Agent" isn't a punchline—it's an architecture. Your agent knows what you know. Mine knows what I know. The protocol connects them.

It's based on transactive memory systems: effective teams don't share all knowledge, they share awareness of *who knows what*. Agents become carriers of that awareness—holding your expertise, establishing credibility, coordinating on your behalf.

The organizations that will thrive aren't designed as machines with humans inside them. They're designed as living systems where talent (human and otherwise) flows to where it's needed most.
