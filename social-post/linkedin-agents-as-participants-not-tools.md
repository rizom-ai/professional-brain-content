We've been asking the wrong question about AI agents.

The choice isn't between obedient tools and autonomous overlords. Both miss what actually matters: agents as genuine participants in how we work.

I've spent years exploring this—from designing distributed communities where AI agents were treated as equal members, to building Rizom as a living ecosystem where professionals and agents work side by side.

The insight that changed everything came from transactive memory systems. Effective teams don't share all knowledge. They share awareness of who knows what. They specialize, build credibility, coordinate.

What if agents worked the same way?

Not as command executors. Not as replacements. But as carriers of expertise—holding your knowledge, establishing trust with others, coordinating on your behalf across a network.

"Have your agent call my agent" sounds like a punchline. It's actually an architecture.

It means your agent knows what you know. My agent knows what I know. The call between them connects our expertise without either of us losing control.

The organizations that will thrive aren't the ones that extract value from AI. They're the ones designed as living systems—where agents and humans participate together in shared ecosystems of knowledge and trust.

The future of work isn't about automation. It's about collaboration at a different scale.
