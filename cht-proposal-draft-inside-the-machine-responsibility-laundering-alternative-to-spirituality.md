## Responsibility Laundering: How AI Agents Are Built to Act Without Answering — and the Open-Source Alternative

**Summary.** The industry is racing to deploy AI agents: systems that don't just answer questions but retrieve, draft, route, prioritize, and act inside organizations. I build these systems. I maintain an open-source agent framework and deploy it with real organizations, which gives me firsthand knowledge of the layer this focus area asks about — how agentic AI is actually wired together, how capability claims should be evaluated against what survives contact with a workplace, and what drives the design decisions that ship. From that seat, the central finding is this: today's agents are institutionally ambiguous *by design*. They sound like analysts, act like coordinators, influence like managers, and disappear like tools. When something goes wrong there is no role that was exceeded, no provenance to inspect, no path for an affected person to contest the output. I call this *responsibility laundering*: agency passes through the machine and comes out clean. This project pairs an insider's anatomy of how that happens with something critique alone cannot supply — a working, open-source proof that the alternative is buildable. CHT's description asks what it would take to shift the incentives shaping this technology from the inside out. One answer: build the accountable version in the open, publish the design standard, and give it away.

**Research questions.**

1. *The anatomy of responsibility laundering.* Which specific design choices in current agent architectures erase answerability? From practice: context-free assistant personas that speak from nowhere; synthesis that strips provenance and source status; permission models that bound what an agent can *access* but never what it is *for*; absent contestation paths. Even when a human approves the output, the agent has already shaped the frame in which the decision became visible.
2. *The capability-claims pipeline.* How does a benchmark result become a sales deck, a procurement decision, and a deployment mandate — and where exactly does critical evaluation fail? This is CHT's question about evaluating capability claims, answered from the layer where the claims land: what a "95% accurate" agent actually does inside a municipality, a foundation, a company.
3. *The economics that select these defaults.* Accountability is friction, and friction loses procurement battles: general frictionless assistants demo better, sell faster, and deploy cheaper than bounded, contestable ones. Failed deployments are rebranded rather than reported, so the public narrative — adopt or fall behind — never meets the base rates. This is the demand-side complement to the financial architecture CHT's description names: the spending only defies business logic until you see what buyers are told.
4. *The buildable alternative — with receipts.* My white paper specifies *situated agents*: named role, bounded scope, provenance-carrying output, explicit escalation and contestation paths. I am building this pattern as [open-source infrastructure](https://github.com/rizom-ai/brains) and running it with real organizations. This project turns that practice into a published design standard precise enough for an engineer to implement and a procurement officer to require — the existence proof that the current defaults are choices, not physics.

**Why these questions are worth answering.** The public conversation about AI agents runs on vendor claims and lab announcements. The layer where those claims become decisions about real people — who gets flagged, prioritized, denied, or framed — is almost undocumented, because the people who see it are selling into it. Most near-term AI harm to ordinary people will be administered through this layer, not through frontier-model misbehavior. A vocabulary that names the mechanism (responsibility laundering, situated agents, ghost managers) gives journalists, regulators, and buyers handles they currently lack; a working reference implementation gives builders something to copy and buyers something to demand.

**Alignment with CHT's framing.** This is "harms are predictable products of business models and design choices" with a paper trail: this feature exists because it demos well; this accountability gap exists because closing it slows the sale. And the constructive orientation is structural, not rhetorical — the deliverable is not only an exposé but a standard plus running code, which is what "the trajectory is not inevitable" looks like when a technologist says it. Full disclosure is part of the design: I sell in this layer, which is exactly why the analysis runs in the open where every claim can be checked.

**Approach.** (a) A technical anatomy of leading agent frameworks and enterprise assistant products, mapping each accountability gap to the design decision that produces it — written to be legible to non-engineers without dumbing down the engineering. (b) Interviews across the deployment ecosystem — engineers shipping agentic systems, implementation consultants, buyers — on the incentives that shape defaults and bury failures. (c) The situated-agent design standard, published openly with the reference implementation. I should be direct about positioning: I am not a frontier-lab insider. I am an insider of the layer where the frontier's claims are monetized and where the public's actual encounter with AI is decided — the kind of technologist perspective this focus area exists to surface.

**Deliverables (6 months).** Flagship report *Responsibility Laundering*; 2–3 op-eds (e.g., "Your New AI Coworker Has No Job Description," "Nobody Reports a Failed AI Project"); a conversation on Your Undivided Attention or another CHT platform — the fellowship's podcast expectation — tracing how one agent-shaped decision travels through an organization and where answerability leaks out. All work happens as open-notebook research in a [public, provenance-tracked knowledge base](https://yeehaa.io/dashboard) running on the same open-source infrastructure the project examines — the research is itself a live demonstration of the design standard it proposes.

## Decision frame: this proposal vs. Spirituality rev 3.1 (for Jan Hein + team)

**For this one (team's view):** brains become subject, not just method — the one proposal only Jan Hein can write; directly answers CHT's "shift incentives from the inside out"; positions him publicly as a technologist, which may serve Rizom beyond the fellowship; maximal coherence across proposals, video, CV, and repo links.

**Against (odds view):** CHT's description literally asks for "leading AI systems" / "inside leading AI companies" insiders or AI-finance experts — a deep, well-networked applicant pool where an ex-lab candidate beats a reframe; conflict-of-interest exposure is sharpest when the product is the subject; Cognition + Machine are both "AI inside organizations," losing the portfolio spread that Cognition + Spirituality had (and Spirituality remains the thinnest applicant pool).

**Mitigations if chosen:** the disclosure is built into Alignment; the honest-positioning sentence ("not a frontier-lab insider") preempts the mismatch objection instead of hiding it; RQ3 leans into the economics angle CHT explicitly invited, which softens the "not ex-lab" gap.

**Note:** applying to two areas costs nothing extra — the real question is which second lane maximizes (a) selection odds, (b) what Jan Hein wants to be known for after. (a) favors Spirituality; (b) may favor Machine. Team decision.
