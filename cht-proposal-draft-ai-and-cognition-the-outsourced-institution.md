## The Outsourced Institution: What Happens When Organizations Offload Memory and Judgment to AI

**Summary.** CHT asks whether AI is more like a calculator or something qualitatively different for human thinking. I propose to answer that question at a level the current debate almost entirely ignores: the institution. Humans have never thought alone — we think through organizations, archives, roles, and procedures that remember, judge, and decide on our behalf. Institutions are now offloading their cognition to AI faster than any individual, and we have almost no rigorous account of what that does to collective judgment. This project extends the cognitive-offloading question from the individual mind to the institutional one, and asks: what happens to a society when its organizations can retrieve everything and remember nothing?

**Research questions.**

1. *Retrieval versus memory.* Institutional memory is not storage; it is the capacity to make the past available to present judgment — knowing which decisions are still alive, which documents are ceremonial, which exceptions became practice. When AI summarization and retrieval substitute for that capacity, what exactly is lost? Can the difference be made observable?
2. *Fluency versus judgment.* AI compresses weak institutional context into confident, fluent output. Under what conditions does that fluency displace the deliberation it imitates — and make institutional gaps harder to see and harder to contest?
3. *Cognitive monoculture.* CHT's description asks what it means for diversity of thought when hundreds of millions of people rely on the same small set of models. The institutional version is sharper: when thousands of organizations route their internal sense-making through identical foundation models, do their judgments converge? What are the systemic risks of correlated institutional cognition — in markets, public administration, media?
4. *The disappearing apprenticeship.* Institutional judgment has historically been transmitted through the humans who carried it: veterans, fixers, coordinators. If AI absorbs their tasks without absorbing their context, how does the next generation of institutional judgment form at all? (This is the adults-versus-kids question in organizational form: what happens to those who never practice the delegated task?)

**Why these questions are worth answering.** The individual-cognition debate has an emerging evidence base; the institutional one has almost none, even though most consequential decisions — medical, financial, governmental, educational — are made by institutions, not individuals. My working hypothesis, developed in the white paper *Institutions That Can Carry Themselves*, is that AI functions as an *institutional stress test*: it does not create cognitive decay in organizations, it exposes and amplifies it, compressing missing context into plausible output. If that is right, harms will surface not as dramatic model failures but as quiet erosion of institutions' capacity to explain, contest, and stand behind their own decisions — a mechanism for institutional distrust usually attributed to politics alone. The field currently runs on anecdote; distinguishing what we know from what we suspect is precisely the work.

**Alignment with CHT's framing.** These outcomes are not inevitable properties of the technology. They are predictable products of design choices (chat interfaces that present synthesis without provenance or status), business models (per-seat productivity tools optimized for output volume, not decision quality), and institutional incentives (throughput metrics that reward fast fluent answers over contestable ones). Naming the mechanism creates the possibility of different choices — interfaces that carry provenance, deployment norms that protect deliberation, procurement standards that ask what a tool does to judgment rather than only to throughput.

**Approach.** (a) A conceptual framework drawing on distributed and extended cognition (Hutchins; Clark and Chalmers), organizational memory research, and my background as a philosopher — establishing precise, usable distinctions between storage/memory and fluency/judgment. (b) Qualitative case studies: structured interviews with practitioners in 6–10 organizations that have deployed AI assistants over internal knowledge (a public agency, a foundation, a mid-size company, a newsroom), tracing concrete decisions before and after adoption. (c) A synthesis of the emerging empirical literature on cognitive offloading, honestly marking where individual-level findings do and do not transfer to institutions.

**Deliverables (6 months).** Flagship report *The Outsourced Institution* — framework, case evidence, and a practical diagnostic ("is this system extending our judgment or replacing it?"); 2–3 op-eds (e.g., "Your Company Doesn't Remember Anything Anymore," "The Coming Monoculture of Institutional Judgment"); a podcast episode on why AI's real cognitive impact may run through our institutions rather than our brains.

**Fit.** Former assistant professor (University of Amsterdam) turned founder; a decade building knowledge and learning infrastructure for organizations, most recently at Rizom. I have watched the offloading happen from inside the deployments, and I can translate between the philosophy of cognition, the empirical literature, and what practitioners actually experience.
