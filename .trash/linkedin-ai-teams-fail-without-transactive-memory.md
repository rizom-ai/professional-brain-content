Most AI implementations fail not because the technology is bad.

They fail because teams don't know who knows what.

When you add an AI system to a team without redesigning how people actually work together, you create chaos. The AI becomes another tool competing for attention instead of a collaborator that amplifies human capability.

I've watched this pattern repeat: teams bolt AI onto existing workflows, then wonder why adoption stalls and trust erodes.

The problem isn't technical. It's organizational.

What works is building what researchers call a "transactive memory system"—a shared map of who has what knowledge, who trusts whom, and how decisions actually get made. When this breaks down, AI becomes noise. When it works, humans and AI operate as a genuine distributed team.

This means:

Clear roles that honor both human judgment and machine pattern-recognition
Explicit trust-building between people and systems
Practices that make knowledge visible across the team
Feedback loops that let everyone learn from each other

The future of work isn't humans replaced by AI or humans fighting AI for control.

It's humans and AI designing their collaboration the way living systems do—with clear boundaries, shared purpose, and mutual accountability.
