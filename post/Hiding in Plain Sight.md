> “The map is not the territory.” — Alfred Korzybski

The strange thing about many institutions is that they are not stupid.

They are, in fact, often full of smart people, careful people, conscientious people, people with deep memory and hard-won judgment. The problem is not absence. The problem is opacity.

What an institution needs most is often already there, quietly embedded in a dozen inboxes, a pile of half-finished docs, a few overworked heads, and one heroic spreadsheet that should probably be illegal. The intelligence exists. The trouble is that it cannot be seen, found, trusted, or reused.

So it behaves as if it does not exist.

This is one of the great little tragedies of organizational life: the most important things are hiding in plain sight.

Knowledge exists, but it is buried in someone’s memory.
Responsibilities exist, but nobody can tell where they begin or end.
Overlap exists, but only becomes visible after a problem has already happened.
Coordination opportunities exist, but they are invisible until a crisis forces everyone into the same room, on the same call, at the same hour, pretending that this was the plan all along.

And then everyone says, with remarkable confidence, that the institution needs more communication.

It usually needs legibility.

That distinction matters. Communication is movement. Legibility is structure. One sends signals; the other makes meaning durable. You can have endless communication and still have no idea where anything lives. You can have a very chatty institution that is, underneath the noise, profoundly unknowable.

I have seen this everywhere: in education, in community projects, in open source, in bootcamps, in the soft, hopeful chaos of people trying to build something together without first building the means to remember what they built yesterday.

In those settings, the hidden cost is not just inefficiency. It is dependence on luck.

Who happens to know the answer?
Who happens to remember the context?
Who happens to be online when the decision gets made?
Who happens to understand the unwritten rule that nobody wrote down because “we all know it already”?

This is how institutions become oddly medieval. Not in aesthetics—though I admit there are enough ceremonial Slack channels to qualify—but in epistemology. Knowledge becomes local, private, and inherited by proximity. If you know the right person, you know the thing. If you were there when the thing was invented, you may continue to rule over the thing. If not, good luck. May your search terms be blessed.

That is not resilience. That is concealed fragility.

When knowledge stays tacit, institutions start mistaking familiarity for shared understanding. A few people become living APIs. Everyone else learns to ask them the same questions again and again, which is flattering for approximately fifteen minutes and then deeply inefficient.

I once wrote about how collaboration is still mostly an accident, and that remains true. But accidents do not happen in a vacuum. They happen because the terrain is illegible. The door is there, but not marked. The path exists, but only if you already know how to walk it. The overlap is real, but invisible. The work is present, but scattered across formats, tools, and memories that refuse to line up.

This is why institutions fail to remember.

Not because memory is gone, but because memory is not held in a form that can survive turnover, drift, or scale. It lives in people who leave. It lives in documents nobody trusts. It lives in meeting lore, in “as you know” emails, in the implicit confidence of those who have been around long enough to mistake pattern recognition for organizational design.

Then the people leave, and the institution performs a small ritual of panic.

New staff arrive and are told to “read the docs,” as though the docs were not written by someone in a hurry, three roles ago, inside a system that had already forgotten why it existed. People spend weeks reconstructing decisions from fragments. They rediscover old debates as if they were ancient tablets. They duplicate work. They solve problems twice. Sometimes three times, if the gods are feeling playful.

The institution is not lacking intelligence.
It is lacking surfaces.

And surfaces matter. A surface is where knowledge becomes usable. It is where the hidden becomes navigable. It is where a distributed group can orient itself without requiring clairvoyance or a direct line to the most exhausted person in the building.

This is why legibility is not a bureaucratic fetish. It is a condition of intelligence.

An unreadable institution cannot fully know itself. It cannot tell what it has, what it needs, what it has already tried, or where the seams are. It cannot learn cleanly, because learning requires comparison, and comparison requires structure. Without legibility, every problem feels new. Every transition feels like reinvention. Every handoff is a loss.

And now, of course, we have AI.

Everyone wants to know what AI will do to organizations, but the more interesting question is what it reveals about them. Large language models are extraordinarily good at surfacing hidden patterns in messy systems. They are also mercilessly dependent on the quality of what they can see. Feed them a swamp and they become a very articulate swamp.

AI does not solve illegibility. It exposes it.

If your institution has knowledge scattered across drives, chats, PDFs, personal notes, and oral tradition, AI will not magically make it coherent. It may, however, make the incoherence obvious. It can help retrieve, summarize, connect, and draft—but only if there is something worth retrieving, summarizing, connecting, and drafting.

In that sense, AI is a legibility machine. Or at least a legibility stress test.

Distributed work does the same thing. Once people are not physically co-located by default, the old tricks stop working. You can no longer rely on ambient awareness, overheard context, or the sacred ritual of “I’ll just swing by your desk.” The institution’s hidden structure comes into view because the hidden structure was doing most of the work all along.

That is the uncomfortable gift of distance: it forces form.

This is why knowledge infrastructure matters. Not as an abstract tech category, but as an institutional capacity. Systems for memory, retrieval, annotation, ownership, and context are not accessories to the real work. They are part of the real work. They determine whether an organization can think across time rather than merely improvise inside the present tense.

I’m interested in this because I’ve lived the alternative. Offcourse, the open learning platform I worked on, taught me quickly that good intentions do not automatically produce cumulative learning. Education projects are often built on a quiet miracle: somehow, the right people remember the right things, and somehow, the next cohort does not start from zero. But “somehow” is not a system. It is a prayer.

Rizom exists because I am tired of prayer as an operating model.

What we need are institutions that can make their own intelligence visible to themselves. Not just documents, but context. Not just storage, but orientation. Not just information, but the ability to recognize what matters, where it belongs, and who should be able to act on it.

Because that is the deeper issue here: hidden knowledge is not neutral.

If only a few people can see the relevant context, then only a few people can exercise judgment. If only a few people can find the right history, then only a few people can make informed decisions. If only a few people understand the overlaps, then coordination becomes a private art. The institution may appear distributed, but power stays concentrated in whoever can navigate the fog.

That is not a talent problem.
It is an architecture problem.

The good news, if there is any, is that legibility can be designed.

Not perfectly. Not once and for all. Not as a holy data model descending from the clouds to absolve us of our habits. But legibility can be cultivated: in the ways knowledge is named, linked, surfaced, and kept alive. In the ways roles are made clear enough to act on. In the ways memory is preserved outside the heads of the already-overloaded.

The institution does not need to become transparent in some naive utopian sense. It needs to become navigable.

That may be the most underrated form of care we have. To make what matters findable. To make the implicit reusable. To make the intelligence of a collective available to the collective.

Because when institutions hide their own knowledge, they do not merely lose efficiency. They lose themselves, one tacit assumption at a time.

And once you notice that, you start seeing it everywhere: the answer already there, waiting to be seen; the structure already present, waiting to be named; the intelligence already alive, hiding in plain sight.
