Most institutions do not introduce AI as authority.

They introduce it as help.

A team is overloaded, so the machine summarizes the meeting. The archive is impossible to navigate, so it finds the relevant documents. The committee has too much to read, so it compares the proposals. The strategy is vague, so it turns fragments into options. The process is slow, so it drafts the next step.

None of this looks dramatic. That is why it matters.

AI rarely enters the institution as a director. It enters as a convenience. It promises to reduce friction in places where the institution is already tired: memory, coordination, judgment, translation, prioritization. It takes on the work people no longer have time, structure, or shared language to do well.

At first, this feels harmless. Often it is genuinely useful.

But roles drift.

A summary becomes the version people remember. A comparison becomes the frame through which options are seen. A recommendation becomes the starting point. A starting point becomes the default. A default becomes the thing no one has the energy to contest.

No one has to announce that authority has moved. The institution simply starts moving around the machine.

This is the real risk. Not that AI becomes human. Not that it wakes up, wants things, or develops a personality. The risk is much more ordinary: an institution gives a system a role without naming the authority that comes with that role.

A machine asked to summarize notes is not playing the same role as a machine asked to rank candidates. A system that retrieves decisions is not the same as a system that decides which decisions matter. A tool that drafts options is not the same as a tool whose options quietly become the agenda.

The difference is not technical. It is institutional.

Institutions have always delegated agency into things. A form decides what kind of person or event can be recognized. A budget makes some futures available and others unrealistic. A calendar teaches an organization what deserves attention. A dashboard quietly defines what counts. A door, a badge, a checklist, a template, a workflow: none of these are neutral surfaces. They shape what people can do.

Bruno Latour was useful because he refused to treat these things as passive scenery. A speed bump tells drivers to slow down. A door closer scripts behavior. A form distributes responsibility before anyone begins to deliberate. Objects participate. They do not merely sit there.

AI intensifies this old institutional habit.

It does not only hold a rule, display a number, or route a document. It can read across traces, produce language, compare alternatives, explain a decision, recommend an action, and appear to know why something matters. It can be assistant, librarian, coach, auditor, analyst, mediator, manager, or judge, depending on where it is placed.

That placement is the point.

The same model can be a useful colleague in one setting and a quiet authority in another. The difference is not only what the system can do. It is what the institution allows its output to become.

This is where Donna Haraway matters. Her great refusal was the view from nowhere: the fantasy that knowledge becomes more trustworthy when it appears to have no body, no position, no history, no stake. But knowledge does not become responsible by pretending to float above the world. It becomes responsible by admitting where it sees from.

Every useful perspective is partial. Every intelligence is situated.

Institutions often forget this, even without AI. Committees hide judgment inside procedure. Managers hide preference inside strategy. Dashboards hide assumptions inside metrics. Experts hide their position behind neutral language. Institutions are very good at laundering situated judgment into impersonal form.

AI makes that laundering easier.

It can make a decision feel less like a decision and more like an output. It can turn disagreement into ranking, politics into procedure, judgment into recommendation. It can speak in a voice that seems to belong to nobody in particular.

That is seductive, especially for institutions that are already struggling to carry their own memory and authority.

An institution that lacks memory will ask AI to become memory. An institution that lacks coordination will ask AI to become the coordinator. An institution that lacks judgment will ask AI to become judgment. An institution that lacks courage will ask AI to become the thing that says no. An institution that lacks accountability will ask AI to become a place where responsibility can disappear.

This is not because the machine is evil. It is because the institution is suggestible.

It wants coordination without negotiation. Memory without maintenance. Judgment without exposure. Authority without anyone having to stand behind it.

AI seems to offer all of this because it speaks fluently. But fluency is not responsibility.

A machine can produce an answer. It cannot be answerable in the institutional sense. It cannot repair trust after a bad call. It cannot sit with the people affected by what it recommended. It cannot explain which silences mattered unless the institution has already found a way to make those silences part of the situation.

That is why the question is not only what AI can do.

The question is what role the institution is asking it to play.

Is it retrieving memory, or deciding what counts as memory? Is it drafting options, or narrowing the field of possible action? Is it helping people prepare for judgment, or replacing the conditions under which judgment can happen? Is it making work more legible, or making authority harder to locate?

A colleague is not valuable because it is human. A colleague is valuable because it is situated.

It knows the work from somewhere. It has limits. It can be corrected. Its contribution can be argued with. It participates in a field of responsibility. It is close enough to the work to be useful and bounded enough not to become sovereign.

That is the role AI should be forced to earn.

Not oracle. Not judge. Not ghost manager. Not director.

A situated colleague.

That means its access has to be named. Its limits have to be visible. Its authority has to be bounded. Its outputs have to remain contestable. Its failures have to be read as institutional signals, not only technical defects. And someone, somewhere, has to remain responsible for what happens after it speaks.

Without that discipline, the machine will know the documents without knowing which ones are ceremonial. It will know the process without knowing the practice. It will know the organization chart without knowing where responsibility actually lives. It will know the archive without knowing which decisions still matter. It will know the vocabulary without knowing what the vocabulary allows people to avoid.

These are not small limitations.

They are the conditions under which institutions make meaning.

A colleague without context does not stay harmless for long. In a healthy institution, its limits are visible. Its outputs contribute to judgment instead of replacing it. Its role is bounded. Its authority is named. People know when to listen, when to argue, and when to ignore it.

In an unhealthy institution, the same limits are hidden. The machine becomes a way to bypass missing context rather than repair it. People ask it for decisions because the decision process is unclear. They ask it for memory because the archive is unusable. They ask it for priorities because the institution has not agreed on what matters. They ask it for neutrality because no one wants to confront disagreement.

Then the problem is no longer that AI lacks context.

The problem is that the institution lacked context first, and the machine has been invited to perform as if that were not true.

New institutions will need a better discipline than adoption.

They will need to decide which roles machines are allowed to play, under what conditions, with what limits, and with whom remaining accountable for the effects. They will need to distinguish assistance from authority, retrieval from memory, contribution from decision, fluency from judgment.

They will need to stop pretending that intelligence becomes safer when it appears to come from nowhere.

The task is not to make AI more human.

The task is to make its institutional role more honest.
