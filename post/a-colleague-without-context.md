There is a small lie at the center of many AI adoptions.

No one says: we are going to move authority into a machine.

They say: we are going to make work easier.

The team is overloaded, so the machine summarizes the meeting. The archive is unusable, so it finds the relevant document. The committee has too much to read, so it compares the proposals. The strategy is vague, so it turns fragments into options. The process is slow, so it drafts the next step.

Each move is reasonable.

Together, they change the institution.

A summary becomes the version people remember. A comparison becomes the frame through which options are seen. A recommendation becomes the place the conversation starts. A starting point becomes the default. A default becomes the thing no one has the energy to contest.

No one has to announce that authority has moved. The institution simply starts moving around the system.

That is why the central question is not whether AI is human-like. It is not whether the machine has intentions, feelings, consciousness, or a secret desire to become a manager. That question is often a distraction.

The more immediate question is institutional: what role has the machine been allowed to play?

A tool that summarizes notes is not doing the same thing as a tool that decides which notes matter. A system that retrieves past decisions is not doing the same thing as a system that ranks which precedents should count. A machine that drafts options is not doing the same thing as a machine whose options quietly become the agenda.

The difference is not intelligence.

The difference is authority.

This is where Donna Haraway is still useful. Her argument against the view from nowhere was never only about science. It was about responsibility. Knowledge does not become more trustworthy by pretending it has no position. It becomes more responsible by admitting where it sees from.

> The moral is simple: only partial perspective promises objective vision.
>
> — Donna Haraway

The problem with many institutional uses of AI is precisely the opposite. The machine is treated as if it speaks from nowhere. Not from a department. Not from a history. Not from a political position. Not from a set of documents selected by someone, structured by someone, omitted by someone, and made legible by someone.

It appears to answer without belonging.

That appearance is powerful. Institutions already love this trick. They hide judgment inside procedure. They hide conflict inside process. They hide values inside metrics. They hide responsibility inside committees, dashboards, and policies. Much of institutional life consists of turning situated decisions into neutral-looking forms.

AI makes that easier.

It can make judgment feel like output. It can make disagreement look like ranking. It can make politics appear as procedure. It can speak in a voice that seems to belong to nobody in particular.

That is seductive, especially in institutions that are already tired.

An institution that lacks memory will ask AI to become memory. An institution that lacks coordination will ask AI to become the coordinator. An institution that lacks judgment will ask AI to become judgment. An institution that lacks courage will ask AI to become the thing that says no.

This is not because the machine is evil.

It is because the institution is suggestible.

It wants coordination without negotiation. Memory without maintenance. Judgment without exposure. Authority without anyone having to stand behind it.

AI seems to offer all of that because it speaks fluently.

But fluency is not responsibility.

A machine can produce an answer. It cannot be answerable in the institutional sense. It cannot repair trust after a bad call. It cannot sit with the people affected by what it recommended. It cannot explain which silences mattered unless the institution has already found a way to make those silences part of the situation.

This does not mean machines have no agency. That would be another comforting fiction. Institutions have always acted through nonhuman things. Forms, files, budgets, calendars, dashboards, buildings, software systems, checklists, credentials, and meeting rooms all shape what people can do. They authorize, delay, exclude, remember, forget, and rank.

Bruno Latour was useful because he refused to treat objects as passive scenery. The social world is not made by humans on one side and inert tools on the other. Power lasts by being delegated into things.

> Technology is society made durable.
>
> — Bruno Latour

A form is never just a form. A dashboard is never just a dashboard. A workflow is never just a workflow. These things carry assumptions about what matters, who counts, what can be seen, and what can be ignored.

AI is part of this older story, but it changes the intensity.

It does not only route a document or display a number. It can read across traces, produce language, compare alternatives, explain patterns, recommend action, and appear to know why something matters. Depending on where it is placed, the same system can become assistant, librarian, analyst, coach, auditor, mediator, manager, or judge.

That placement is the institutional decision.

Too often, it is not treated as one.

The machine is added to the workflow as if the workflow were already clear. It is connected to the archive as if the archive were already memory. It is asked to support decisions as if the decision process were already accountable. It is asked to summarize disagreement as if the institution still knew how to disagree.

Then everyone is surprised when the AI reveals more confusion than clarity.

But the confusion was already there.

The machine only made it harder to hide.

A colleague is not valuable because it is human. A colleague is valuable because it is situated. It knows the work from somewhere. It has limits. It can be corrected. Its contribution can be argued with. It belongs to a field of responsibility. It is close enough to the work to be useful and bounded enough not to become sovereign.

That is the role AI should be forced to earn.

Not oracle. Not judge. Not ghost manager. Not director.

A situated colleague.

That means its access has to be named. Its limits have to be visible. Its authority has to be bounded. Its outputs have to remain contestable. And someone, somewhere, has to remain responsible for what happens after it speaks.

Without that discipline, the machine will know the documents without knowing which ones are ceremonial. It will know the process without knowing the practice. It will know the organization chart without knowing where responsibility actually lives. It will know the archive without knowing which decisions still matter. It will know the vocabulary without knowing what that vocabulary allows people to avoid.

These are not small limitations.

They are the conditions under which institutions make meaning.

In a healthy institution, AI's limits become useful. A bad answer reveals a missing distinction. A vague summary reveals an unclear decision. A failed retrieval reveals an archive that no longer carries context. The machine becomes part of the institution's learning because its role is bounded and its failures can be interpreted.

In an unhealthy institution, the same limits disappear from view. The machine becomes a way to bypass missing context rather than repair it. People ask it for decisions because the decision process is unclear. They ask it for memory because the archive is unusable. They ask it for priorities because the institution has not agreed on what matters. They ask it for neutrality because no one wants to confront disagreement.

Then the problem is no longer that AI lacks context.

The problem is that the institution lacked context first, and the machine has been invited to perform as if that were not true.

New institutions will need a better discipline than adoption.

They will need to decide which roles machines are allowed to play, under what conditions, with what limits, and with whom remaining accountable for the effects. They will need to distinguish assistance from authority, retrieval from memory, contribution from decision, fluency from judgment.

The point is not to make AI more human.

The point is to stop pretending that intelligence becomes safer when it appears to come from nowhere.

A colleague without context is not a colleague for long.

It becomes a way for the institution to avoid seeing itself.
