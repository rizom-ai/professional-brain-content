> "The forest is not just trees. The forest is a complex system." — Suzanne Simard

We spent years at Offcourse trying to optimize the learning experience. Better curriculum design, smarter feedback loops, more efficient pathways. All very rational. All very machine-like. And it worked until it didn't—the moment we needed the system to adapt, to heal, to actually grow in unexpected directions. That's when I realized: we were thinking about organizations wrong.

The problem isn't that we lack good ideas. It's that our mental models are fundamentally industrial. We inherited them from factory floors and military hierarchies, and we've been applying them to problems that require something different. We talk about "scaling" like it's a virtue, "efficiency" like it's an end goal, "disruption" like it's progress.

But forests don't scale. They grow. They don't optimize; they adapt. They don't have five-year plans; they respond to seasons, to drought, to the mycorrhizal networks beneath the soil carrying nutrients and information between trees that seem separate but are actually deeply connected.

This is what ecosystem architecture actually means. Not some trendy rebranding of management theory. Not "we're decentralized now, we're web3." But fundamentally rethinking how we structure organizations to work more like living systems. Which means understanding that the health of the whole depends on relationships you can't see on an org chart. It means accepting that some of the most important work happens in the spaces between roles, not within them.

When I was building Rizom, this shifted everything. Instead of asking "how do we make knowledge management more efficient?" I started asking "what conditions allow knowledge to flow naturally?" The difference is subtle but total. One is about control and extraction. The other is about cultivation and emergence.

The future of work isn't more automation or better tools, though we need those. It's learning to think like forests instead of factories. To understand that the most resilient organizations are the ones that look a little wild, where unexpected connections happen, where people have room to grow in directions nobody planned for.

That's harder to measure. It doesn't fit neatly on a slide. But it's the only way anything actually survives.
