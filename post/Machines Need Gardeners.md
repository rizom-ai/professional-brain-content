> "The machine does not isolate man from the great problems of nature but plunges him more deeply into them." — Marshall McLuhan

We've been building organizations like engineers, not gardeners. Straight lines, clear hierarchies, predictable outputs. It made sense when the world moved slower and change came in decades instead of quarters. But somewhere along the way, we got stuck with a metaphor that stopped working.

I noticed this first with Offcourse. We built an open-source learning platform with the best intentions—decentralized, modular, rational. But the moment real humans showed up, the system revealed its mechanical bones. It could distribute content but not cultivate understanding. It could track progress but not nurture growth. The design assumed learners were inputs to be processed, not living systems with their own momentum and mystery.

The problem wasn't the technology. It was the thinking underneath it.

When you design a machine, you optimize for efficiency. When you tend a garden, you optimize for resilience. A machine fails catastrophically when one part breaks. A garden adapts. A machine has a designer who controls outcomes. A garden has a gardener who creates conditions and responds to what emerges. These aren't small differences—they're fundamentally different relationships to the world.

This is what ecosystem architecture actually means. Not "decentralization" as a buzzword, but a genuine shift in how we think about organizing. Instead of asking "how do we control this system?" we ask "what conditions allow this system to flourish?" Instead of building rigid structures, we cultivate patterns. Instead of optimizing for predictability, we design for antifragility.

Rizom is built on this conviction. Not a platform that dictates how knowledge flows, but infrastructure that enables people to create their own flows. Not a hierarchy that controls who can contribute, but a network that strengthens with participation. It's messier than a machine. It requires constant attention. But it grows in ways no blueprint could predict.

The irony is that gardeners have always known this. Indigenous land management, permaculture, mycological networks—these aren't new ideas. We just forgot them when we fell in love with machines. Now we're remembering, slowly, that the most resilient systems in nature aren't engineered. They're cultivated.

The future of work, of organizations, of knowledge systems—it all depends on whether we're willing to become gardeners instead of engineers.
