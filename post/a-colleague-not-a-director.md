> “All that is solid melts into air.” — Karl Marx

AI does not enter institutions as a neutral tool.
It arrives dressed as a role.

That distinction matters more than the usual chatter about models, accuracy, or speed.
An institution is not just a stack of software and policies.
It is a choreography of responsibilities, permissions, and excuses.
So when AI shows up, the first question is not what it can do.
The first question is what we are asking it to become.

This is where things get slippery.
The same system can be an assistant, a colleague, an auditor, a manager, a judge, or a director.
Not because the model has developed a taste for middle management, but because institutions project function onto whatever is available.
If a place lacks memory, AI becomes memory.
If a place lacks judgment, AI becomes judgment.
If a place lacks courage, AI becomes the thing that says no.

And institutions are often desperate.
They are tired, overworked, under-staffed, and full of procedural theater.
They love a tool that can be made to look objective while quietly absorbing responsibility.
That is the seduction of the oracle.
Ask it a question, receive an answer, pretend the answer came from nowhere.
Very clean. Very efficient. Very dangerous.

Because once AI is given the role of oracle, people stop asking who is accountable for the question.
Once it becomes judge, people begin to confuse output with legitimacy.
Once it becomes manager, workers start receiving instructions from a fog bank.
Once it becomes a surveillance layer, the institution starts calling paranoia “process improvement.”
And once it becomes a ghost director, the whole place begins to move according to an authority nobody can actually confront.

This is not a technical failure.
It is an institutional habit.
We keep assigning machines to unhealthy roles because those roles are already broken in human form.
A bad manager becomes a bad model.
A fake-neutral committee becomes a fake-neutral classifier.
A culture that avoids responsibility will naturally adore automation that also avoids responsibility.

So yes, AI can be useful.
But usefulness is not the issue.
The issue is role design.

I think about this often when I look at organizations trying to “add AI” as if they were installing a new printer.
That language already gives the game away.
You do not install AI into a healthy institution.
You introduce it into an existing social order, where it will be rewarded for whatever the institution values most.
If the institution values speed over thought, the machine will be speed.
If it values compliance over discernment, the machine will be compliance.
If it values plausible deniability, the machine will be the perfect accomplice.

What we need, then, are healthier roles.
Not fantasy roles. Not marketing roles.
Actual roles with boundaries.
A colleague, not a director.
An assistant, not a sovereign.
A librarian, not an oracle.
A translator, not a priest.
A steward, not a ruler.
A second pair of eyes, not the final eye.

These are modest roles, and that is precisely why they matter.
A colleague can be helpful without pretending to be infallible.
A librarian can retrieve, connect, and preserve without deciding what is true for everyone.
A translator can reduce friction without claiming authority over meaning.
A steward can maintain continuity without taking ownership away from the people who actually carry the work.

But even the healthier roles are not automatic.
A colleague still needs context.
A librarian still needs curation.
A steward still needs governance.
And every one of them needs a human institution that knows how to remain responsible.
Otherwise “colleague” becomes “almost-manager,” which is just manager with better branding.

This is where my own thinking keeps returning to structure.
I came to technology through philosophy, and philosophy is annoyingly good at noticing when language is doing institutional work.
Call something an assistant and you are making a claim about hierarchy.
Call it a copilot and you are suggesting shared control, even when the controls are very much not shared.
Call it neutral and you have already hidden the politics.

At Rizom, this question shows up in a very practical way.
If knowledge infrastructure is meant to help organizations work like living systems, then AI cannot be treated as a command center.
Living systems do not thrive when every function is centralized in a single nervous breakdown.
They thrive through distributed sensing, local responsibility, and reciprocal feedback.
AI can participate in that ecology.
It cannot replace it.

And that means the institution has to stay awake.
It has to decide what counts as advice, what counts as judgment, and what counts as action.
It has to know where the machine is allowed to speak and where it must only assist.
It has to resist the convenience of pretending that an answer is the same thing as a decision.

Because the temptation is always to use AI as a shortcut around institutional weakness.
But that shortcut is usually a hallway into deeper weakness.
If the institution cannot name who is accountable, the machine will not solve that.
If the institution cannot remember its own decisions, the machine will not magically give it memory.
If the institution cannot disagree in public, the machine will not create healthy conflict.
It will only accelerate whatever dysfunction was already waiting for a costume.

So the real question is not “What can AI do?”
The real question is: what are we asking it to become?

If we ask it to be a director, we should not be surprised when responsibility evaporates.
If we ask it to be a colleague, we still have work to do.
But at least the work remains human, shared, and answerable.
That is a better place to begin.

Because once the machine has been given a role, it will do what institutions always do: reveal themselves.
And if that role is unhealthy, it will not merely misbehave.
It will find the cracks.
