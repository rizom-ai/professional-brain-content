---
title: 'Transactive Memory Systems: Why Most AI Teams Fail (And How to Fix It)'
---
> "The more I know, the less I understand" — Don Toliver

We've been thinking about AI teams all wrong. We obsess over the AI—its training, its reasoning, its alignment—when the real problem is much older: *trust in who knows what*.

I learned this the hard way building distributed teams at Offcourse. We'd hire brilliant people, scatter them across continents, and watch collaboration collapse not because they lacked skill but because nobody knew who should be asked what. The developer didn't know the designer had already solved the UX problem. The educator didn't know the engineer had strong opinions about pedagogy. Knowledge existed everywhere and nowhere simultaneously.

This is what researchers call a **transactive memory system** (TMS)—the shared understanding of who knows what in a group. It's not about individual intelligence. It's about collective knowledge architecture. When a couple has been together thirty years, they don't need to explain things; they know intuitively who remembers the directions, who knows the recipe, who understands the family dynamics. That's TMS working. It's why breaking up a high-performing team is catastrophic even if you keep the same people. You've destroyed the memory system.

Most AI implementations fail at exactly this point. We introduce an AI agent into a team and nobody knows: Does the AI understand context? Should I ask it or the human expert? Who's responsible if this goes wrong? The team's transactive memory gets scrambled. Trust collapses.

The teams that actually work with AI—I've seen this at Rizom and in the distributed communities we're building—are the ones that *explicitly design for transactive memory*. They document not just what was decided, but *who decided it and why*. They create shared models of expertise: "Sarah understands the customer psychology, the AI understands the data patterns, I understand the product constraints." Not in a rigid org chart way, but as a living understanding that evolves.

In distributed teams especially, this becomes critical. You can't rely on overhearing conversations or office politics to build intuitive knowledge of expertise. You have to be intentional. The best distributed teams I've worked with treat knowledge architecture as seriously as code architecture. They use tools (wikis, decision logs, project histories) not as bureaucratic overhead but as the *substrate for trust*. When someone new joins, they can read not just the what but the who-decided-what, and suddenly they can participate in the transactive memory instead of fumbling in the dark.

With AI, this is even more important. The AI is a new team member with genuinely alien expertise—it sees patterns humans miss, but it also hallucinates confidently and lacks context. A well-designed TMS acknowledges this explicitly. "The AI is good at synthesis and pattern-matching. It's terrible at stakes and consequences. When something matters, we verify with human judgment." Not as a rule imposed from above, but as shared knowledge everyone understands and trusts.

The future of work isn't about replacing humans with AI or vice versa. It's about building teams where the transactive memory is strong enough to include both. Where everyone—human and AI—knows what everyone else knows, and more importantly, knows what they *don't* know. That's when collaboration stops being friction and becomes flow.

The irony is that this isn't new. It's just that we've forgotten how to build it at scale. Your grandmother's village had perfect transactive memory. We've spent a century fragmenting it with organizations that treat knowledge as individual property instead of collective infrastructure. Now we're trying to add AI to broken systems and wondering why it doesn't work.

Fix the memory system first. The AI will follow.
